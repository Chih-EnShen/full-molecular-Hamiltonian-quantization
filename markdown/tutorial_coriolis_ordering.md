# Why the Coriolis operator ends up as Eq. (4.35): a walkthrough

This note explains, at "first-year quantum mechanics" level, one specific step
of Section 4: why the rotation–vibration (Coriolis) part of the kinetic
operator, after reweighting to the Watson measure, comes out as the clean
symmetric form

$$
\hat T_{{\rm Cor},W}
= -\tfrac12\Bigl(\hat J_\alpha\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat\pi_\beta
+ \hat\pi_\alpha\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat J_\beta\Bigr)
\qquad\text{— Eq. (4.35) —}
$$

**with no leftover potential-like term whatsoever.** This is remarkable if you
compare with the vibrational block, which *does* leave behind a multiplicative
residue (part of the Watson pseudopotential — see the companion tutorial
`tutorial_watson_pseudopotential.md`). We will see exactly why the Coriolis
block gets away clean: two small "quantum correction" terms are generated by
the reweighting, and they cancel each other **exactly**.

The plan (fully self-contained — you never need the paper open):

1. Toolkit: four two-line lemmas.
2. Where the Coriolis block (4.30) comes from: expanding the kinetic operator.
3. Why the two groups in (4.30) are adjoints of each other.
4. The reweighting $\psi_W=\gamma^{1/4}\psi$: watch the $\pm\frac{i\hbar}{4}$
   terms appear and annihilate → boxed Eq. (4.35).
5. Why *this* block produces no pseudopotential while the vibrational one does.
6. FAQ.

**Assumed background:** derivatives, product rule, integration by parts, what
"Hermitian operator" means. That's all.

**Notation** (matching the paper):

| Symbol | Meaning | Depends on / acts on |
|---|---|---|
| $\Omega=(\phi,\theta,\chi)$ | Euler angles | — |
| $Q_b,\ b=1,\dots,f$ | normal coordinates | — |
| $\hat J_\alpha=-i\hbar\sum_s(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\partial_{\Omega_s}$ | rotational operators, Eq. (4.24) | Euler angles only |
| $\hat P_b=-i\hbar\,\partial_{Q_b}$ | vibrational momentum | $Q$ only |
| $\hat\pi_\alpha=\sum_b\sigma_{\alpha b}(Q)\,\hat P_b$ | vibrational angular momentum | $Q$ only |
| $\sigma_{\alpha b}=\sum_a\zeta^{\alpha}_{ab}Q_a$ | Coriolis vectors, **linear** in $Q$, constant $\zeta$'s | multiplication |
| $\mu_{\alpha\beta}(Q)$ | Watson reciprocal inertia, real **symmetric** | multiplication |
| $\gamma(Q)=\det\mathbf I'$ | shape part of the Jacobian | multiplication |
| $\sqrt g=\sin\theta\,\sqrt{\gamma}$ | full Jacobian / volume weight, Eq. (4.21) | — |

Greek indices $\alpha,\beta\in\{1,2,3\}$, mode indices $b,c\in\{1,\dots,f\}$;
repeated indices are summed.

Two structural facts we lean on constantly:

- $\hat J_\alpha$ touches **only** Euler angles; $\mu,\sigma,\gamma$ depend
  **only** on $Q$. Operators and functions living in different coordinate
  sectors commute. So $\hat J_\alpha$ slides freely past every $Q$-function.
- $\hat P_b$ does **not** commute with $Q$-functions:
  $\hat P_b\,f(Q) = f(Q)\,\hat P_b - i\hbar\,(\partial_{Q_b}f)$. This one
  product rule is the source of everything interesting below.

## 1. Toolkit: four small lemmas

### Lemma A — reweighting conjugates operators

If you change the wavefunction by a positive function,
$\psi_W \equiv \gamma^{1/4}\psi$, then every operator $\hat O$ acting on
$\psi$ becomes $\gamma^{1/4}\hat O\,\gamma^{-1/4}$ acting on $\psi_W$. (Check:
$\hat O\psi = \gamma^{-1/4}\,[\gamma^{1/4}\hat O\gamma^{-1/4}]\,\psi_W$.)

### Lemma B — what conjugation does to $\hat P_b$

Compute directly with the product rule:

$$
\gamma^{1/4}\,\hat P_b\,\gamma^{-1/4}\,\psi
= -i\hbar\,\gamma^{1/4}\,\partial_{Q_b}\!\bigl(\gamma^{-1/4}\psi\bigr)
= -i\hbar\Bigl[\partial_{Q_b}\psi
   - \tfrac14\bigl(\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma\bigr)\psi\Bigr],
$$

so

$$
\boxed{\;\gamma^{1/4}\,\hat P_b\,\gamma^{-1/4}
= \hat P_b + \tfrac{i\hbar}{4}\,\bigl(\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma\bigr),
\qquad
\gamma^{-1/4}\,\hat P_b\,\gamma^{+1/4}
= \hat P_b - \tfrac{i\hbar}{4}\,\bigl(\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma\bigr).\;}
$$

(For the second one, run the same computation with the signs of the exponents
flipped.) These **opposite-sign shifts** are the whole story of this tutorial.

### Lemma C — the adjoint on a weighted measure

Let the inner product carry a weight, $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle_w=\int
\phi^*\psi\,w\,dQ$. If $\hat A^{\dagger}$ denotes the adjoint on the *flat*
measure ($w=1$), then the adjoint on the weighted one is

$$
\hat A^{\dagger_w} = w^{-1}\,\hat A^{\dagger}\,w .
$$

*Proof (one line):* $\int\phi^*(\hat A\psi)\,w
=\int(w\phi)^*\hat A\psi
=\int(\hat A^{\dagger}w\phi)^*\psi
=\int\bigl(w^{-1}\hat A^{\dagger}w\,\phi\bigr)^*\psi\,w$. $\square$

### Lemma D — $\hat P_b$ slides past $\sigma_{\alpha b}$ *when $b$ is summed*

$\sigma_{\alpha b}=\sum_a\zeta^{\alpha}_{ab}Q_a$ with constant, antisymmetric
$\zeta^{\alpha}_{ab}=-\zeta^{\alpha}_{ba}$ [Eq. (3.17a)]. Therefore
$\partial_{Q_b}\sigma_{\alpha b}=\zeta^{\alpha}_{bb}=0$ (no sum), and

$$
\sum_b\hat P_b\,\sigma_{\alpha b}
= \sum_b\sigma_{\alpha b}\hat P_b
  \;-\; i\hbar\underbrace{\sum_b\zeta^{\alpha}_{bb}}_{=\,0}
= \hat\pi_\alpha .
$$

So the *summed* combination $\sum_b\hat P_b\,\sigma_{\alpha b}$ **is**
$\hat\pi_\alpha$, whichever side of $\sigma$ the momentum sits on.

## 2. Where the Coriolis block comes from

The exact kinetic operator is the flat-space Laplacian expressed in the
$(\Omega,Q)$ chart [Eq. (4.5)]:

$$
\hat T=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2}\,\frac{1}{\sqrt g}\,
\partial_A\bigl(\sqrt g\,g^{AB}\,\partial_B\bigr),
\qquad \sqrt g=\sin\theta\sqrt\gamma .
$$

The index pair $(A,B)$ runs over Euler angles and modes; the **cross (Coriolis)
block** consists of the mixed terms, with the off-diagonal inverse-metric
blocks $g^{\Omega Q}_{sb}=-(\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma)_{sb}$
and its transpose [Eq. (4.22)]:

$$
\hat T_{\rm Cor}
= -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2\sqrt g}\Bigl[
\underbrace{\partial_{\Omega_s}\bigl(\sqrt g\,g^{\Omega Q}_{sb}\,\partial_{Q_b}\bigr)}_{\text{group 1}}
+\underbrace{\partial_{Q_b}\bigl(\sqrt g\,g^{Q\Omega}_{bs}\,\partial_{\Omega_s}\bigr)}_{\text{group 2}}\Bigr].
$$

### Group 1: the Euler derivative acts first

Inside group 1 sit two kinds of factors: $Q$-functions
($\sqrt\gamma,\mu,\sigma$) that the Euler derivative $\partial_{\Omega_s}$
ignores, and the $\Omega$-functions $\sin\theta\,(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}$
that it acts on. Pull the $Q$-functions out and let the $\sqrt\gamma$ cancel
against the prefactor:

$$
\text{group 1}
= +\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2}\,\frac{1}{\sin\theta}\,
\partial_{\Omega_s}\Bigl[\sin\theta\,(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\Bigr]\,
(\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma)_{\alpha b}\,\partial_{Q_b}
\;+\;\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2}\,(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\,
(\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma)_{\alpha b}\,\partial_{\Omega_s}\partial_{Q_b}.
$$

(The overall sign flipped because $g^{\Omega Q}$ carries a minus.) Now the
**divergence identity** [Eq. (4.26)] kills the first piece:

$$
\sum_s\partial_{\Omega_s}\bigl[\sin\theta\,(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\bigr]=0
\qquad\text{for each }\alpha=1,2,3 .
$$

You can verify this by hand from the explicit matrix (4.12). The $\alpha=3$
case is one line — the third column of $\mathsf E^{-1}$ is $(0,0,1)$, so the
sum is $\partial_\chi\sin\theta=0$. For $\alpha=1$:

$$
\partial_\phi\bigl(-\cos\chi\bigr)
+\partial_\theta\bigl(\sin\theta\sin\chi\bigr)
+\partial_\chi\bigl(\cos\theta\cos\chi\bigr)
= 0+\cos\theta\sin\chi-\cos\theta\sin\chi=0 . \checkmark
$$

What survives is the second piece, and comparing with the definition
$\hat J_\alpha=-i\hbar(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\partial_{\Omega_s}$ and
$\hat P_b=-i\hbar\partial_{Q_b}$ (two factors of $-i\hbar$ give $-\hbar^2$):

$$
\text{group 1}
= -\tfrac12\,\hat J_\alpha\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\sigma_{\beta b}\,\hat P_b
= -\tfrac12\,\hat J_\alpha\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat\pi_\beta .
$$

### Group 2: the mode derivative acts first

Now the roles reverse: $\partial_{Q_b}$ ignores $\sin\theta$ and
$\mathsf E^{-1}$ (they are $\Omega$-functions, and they pass through) but
**acts** on $\sqrt\gamma$, $\boldsymbol\sigma$, and $\boldsymbol\mu$. The
$\sin\theta$ cancels against the prefactor, but the $\sqrt\gamma$ does *not* —
it is trapped inside the derivative:

$$
\text{group 2}
= -\tfrac12\,\frac{1}{\sqrt\gamma}\,\hat P_b\,
\sqrt\gamma\;\sigma_{\alpha b}\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat J_\beta .
$$

(Here $\hat P_b$ is understood to differentiate everything to its right,
including $\sqrt\gamma\,\sigma\mu$ and the wavefunction.) Together:

$$
\boxed{\;\hat T_{\rm Cor}
=-\tfrac12\Bigl[\hat J_\alpha\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat\pi_\beta
+\frac{1}{\sqrt\gamma}\,\hat P_b\,\sqrt\gamma\;
\sigma_{\alpha b}\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat J_\beta\Bigr]
\qquad\text{— Eq. (4.30).}\;}
$$

The two groups look annoyingly asymmetric — one clean, one dressed in
$\gamma^{\pm 1/2}$. The next section explains why that dressing is not an
accident.

## 3. The two groups are each other's adjoints

Claim: on the physical measure $\sin\theta\,d\Omega\;\sqrt\gamma\,dQ$,

$$
\Bigl(\hat J_\alpha\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat\pi_\beta\Bigr)^{\dagger}
= \frac{1}{\sqrt\gamma}\,\hat P_b\,\sqrt\gamma\;
\sigma_{\alpha b}\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat J_\beta .
$$

*Proof.* Take the adjoint factor by factor (the order reverses):
$\hat J_\alpha^\dagger=\hat J_\alpha$ on the Haar measure $\sin\theta\,d\Omega$
— that is exactly what the divergence identity buys you, via one integration
by parts [Eq. (4.27)]. For the $Q$-side, $\hat\pi$ is Hermitian on the *flat*
$dQ$ measure (Lemma D makes the ordering irrelevant), so by **Lemma C** its
adjoint on the weighted measure $w=\sqrt\gamma$ is

$$
\hat\pi_\beta^{\dagger_{\sqrt\gamma}}
= \gamma^{-1/2}\,\hat\pi_\beta\,\gamma^{1/2}
= \frac{1}{\sqrt\gamma}\,\sigma_{\beta b}\hat P_b\,\sqrt\gamma
\;\overset{\text{Lemma D}}{=}\;
\frac{1}{\sqrt\gamma}\,\hat P_b\,\sqrt\gamma\,\sigma_{\beta b}\;(\cdots).
$$

Reversing the order of $(\hat J\mu\hat\pi)$ and relabeling the summed pair
$\alpha\leftrightarrow\beta$ (allowed; then use $\mu_{\beta\alpha}
=\mu_{\alpha\beta}$) gives precisely group 2. $\square$

**Consequence:** $\hat T_{\rm Cor}=-\tfrac12(\hat X+\hat X^\dagger)$ is
Hermitian *automatically* — the Laplacian handed us the symmetrized
combination without being asked. No ordering was ever chosen by hand.

## 4. The reweighting: two shifts, one cancellation

Now move to the Watson measure. The Jacobian's shape factor is absorbed into
the wavefunction, $\psi_W=\gamma^{1/4}\psi$ [Eq. (4.32)], and by **Lemma A**
every operator gets conjugated. Apply this to both groups of (4.30).

**Group 1.** Only $\hat P_b$ notices the conjugation ($\hat J,\mu,\sigma$
commute with $\gamma$). By **Lemma B**, first line:

$$
\gamma^{1/4}\Bigl[-\tfrac12\hat J_\alpha\mu_{\alpha\beta}\sigma_{\beta b}\hat P_b\Bigr]\gamma^{-1/4}
= -\tfrac12\hat J_\alpha\mu_{\alpha\beta}\sigma_{\beta b}\hat P_b
\;-\;\frac{i\hbar}{8}\,\hat J_\alpha\,
\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\sigma_{\beta b}\bigl(\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma\bigr) .
$$

**Group 2.** Here the conjugation *merges with the dressing*: the prefactors
combine as $\gamma^{1/4}\cdot\gamma^{-1/2}=\gamma^{-1/4}$ on the left and
$\gamma^{1/2}\cdot\gamma^{-1/4}=\gamma^{1/4}$ inside, so the operator string
becomes $\gamma^{-1/4}\hat P_b\,\gamma^{1/4}$ — **Lemma B, second line**, the
shift with the *opposite sign*:

$$
\gamma^{1/4}\Bigl[-\tfrac12\tfrac{1}{\sqrt\gamma}\hat P_b\sqrt\gamma\,\sigma_{\alpha b}\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat J_\beta\Bigr]\gamma^{-1/4}
= -\tfrac12\,\hat P_b\,\sigma_{\alpha b}\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat J_\beta
\;+\;\frac{i\hbar}{8}\bigl(\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma\bigr)\,
\sigma_{\alpha b}\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat J_\beta .
$$

**The cancellation.** Look at the two $\frac{i\hbar}{8}$ terms. Both are
(function of $Q$) × (one $\hat J$). Since $\hat J$ commutes with every
$Q$-function, slide it to the same side and compare the functions:

$$
\hat J_\alpha\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\sigma_{\beta b}\,\partial_b\ln\gamma
\quad\text{vs.}\quad
\bigl(\partial_b\ln\gamma\bigr)\,\sigma_{\alpha b}\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat J_\beta
= \hat J_\beta\,\underbrace{\mu_{\beta\alpha}}_{=\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}}\,\sigma_{\alpha b}\,\partial_b\ln\gamma .
$$

After relabeling the summed indices they are the **same function multiplying
the same operator** — one enters with $-\frac{i\hbar}{8}$, the other with
$+\frac{i\hbar}{8}$:

$$
-\frac{i\hbar}{8}\,\hat J_\alpha(\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma\,\partial\ln\gamma)_\alpha
+\frac{i\hbar}{8}\,\hat J_\alpha(\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma\,\partial\ln\gamma)_\alpha
= 0 .
$$

**Tidy up.** What remains is
$-\tfrac12\hat J\mu\hat\pi - \tfrac12\hat P_b\,\sigma_{\alpha b}\mu\hat J$;
in the second term use **Lemma D** to slide $\hat P_b$ past $\sigma_{\alpha b}$
(costless under the $b$-sum), giving $\hat\pi_\alpha\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat
J_\beta$. Final result:

$$
\boxed{\;\hat T_{{\rm Cor},W}
= -\tfrac12\Bigl(\hat J_\alpha\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat\pi_\beta
+ \hat\pi_\alpha\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat J_\beta\Bigr)
\qquad\text{— Eq. (4.35), no residue.}\;}
$$

That is the whole proof. The mechanism in one sentence:

> The conjugation shifts $\hat P_b$ by $+\frac{i\hbar}{4}\partial_b\ln\gamma$
> in one group and by $-\frac{i\hbar}{4}\partial_b\ln\gamma$ in the other —
> because the $\gamma^{1/4}$'s approach the momentum *from opposite sides* —
> and since a lone $\hat J$ passes freely through $Q$-functions, the two
> shifted pieces meet and annihilate.

## 5. Why no pseudopotential from this block?

Compare with the vibrational block $\hat T_{\rm vib}$, where the *same*
conjugation **does** leave behind the multiplicative residues $U_{\rm vib}$
and $U_{\rm Cor}$ that build the Watson pseudopotential. The structural
difference:

| | Coriolis block | Vibrational block |
|---|---|---|
| derivative slots | one $\partial_\Omega$, one $\partial_Q$ | **two** $\partial_Q$ |
| shifts from Lemma B | one $+\frac{i\hbar}{4}$, one $-\frac{i\hbar}{4}$ | the $\gamma^{\pm1/4}$'s hit the string $\gamma^{-1/4}\hat P\,\gamma^{1/2}\hat P\,\gamma^{-1/4}$ **twice**, second-derivative and squared terms of $\ln\gamma$ survive |
| residue | none — the shifts cancel | $U=\frac{\hbar^2}{8}\bigl[\partial^2\ln\gamma+\tfrac14(\partial\ln\gamma)^2+\dots\bigr]$ |

Also note *what kind of term* a Coriolis residue would have to be: everything
left over still carries one $\hat J$. A "pseudopotential" is a pure
multiplicative function — and no such term can appear from a block whose every
term keeps an Euler-angle derivative. The paper says this in one line below
Eq. (4.35); the algebra above is that line unpacked.

A second, slicker way to see the whole result is the Podolsky sandwich
[Eq. (4.32′)]: the Coriolis part of the sandwich is
$-\tfrac12\gamma^{-1/4}\bigl[\hat J\gamma^{1/2}(\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma)\hat P
+\hat P\gamma^{1/2}(\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu)\hat J\bigr]\gamma^{-1/4}$,
which is of the form $\hat X+\hat X^\dagger$ *on the Watson measure* — you can
read off Hermiticity before doing any algebra, and the computation of
Section 4 above is just the normal-ordering of this sandwich.

## 6. FAQ

**Q: Where exactly did I use that $\mu$ is symmetric?**
Once, in the cancellation step (Section 4), to identify the two
$\frac{i\hbar}{8}$ functions after relabeling; and once in Section 3, to show
the adjoint of group 1 is group 2 rather than something new.

**Q: Where did I use the antisymmetry of the Coriolis constants
$\zeta^{\alpha}_{ab}$?**
Only through Lemma D ($\zeta^{\alpha}_{bb}=0$), which lets $\hat P_b$ and
$\sigma_{\alpha b}$ trade places under the $b$-sum. Without it,
$\hat\pi^\dagger\neq\hat\pi$ on the flat mode measure and the bookkeeping
would need explicit symmetrization.

**Q: Does the cancellation depend on the specific $\gamma=\det\mathbf I'$ or
the Eckart conditions?**
No — Section 4 works for *any* positive weight $\gamma(Q)$ and any
$\sigma_{\alpha b}(Q)$ with $\sum_b\partial_b\sigma_{\alpha b}=0$. The Eckart
magic is needed only later, to collapse the *vibrational* residues into
Watson's trace (see the companion tutorial).

**Q: Why is the symmetric form (4.35) "the correct" quantization of the
classical $-2J\mu\pi$?**
Because a single ordering $\hat J\mu\hat\pi$ is not Hermitian
($\hat\pi$ does not commute with $\mu(Q)$), while $\hat X+\hat X^\dagger$ is
Hermitian by construction. The deeper point of this tutorial: we never had to
*impose* the symmetric ordering — the flat-space Laplacian produced it on its
own (Section 3), and the reweighting preserved it (Section 4).

**Q: One-sentence summary?**
The Coriolis block is a sum of two mutually adjoint groups; reweighting by
$\gamma^{1/4}$ shifts the momentum by $\pm\frac{i\hbar}{4}\partial\ln\gamma$
with opposite signs in the two groups, the shifts cancel, and the symmetric
kernel $-\tfrac12(\hat J\mu\hat\pi+\hat\pi\mu\hat J)$ is all that survives.
