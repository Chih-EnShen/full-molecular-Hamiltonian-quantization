\section{Quantization}

The classical internal Hamiltonian (3.22) is parametrized by curvilinear coordinates (Euler angles $\phi,\theta,\chi$ and vibrational normal coordinates $Q_b$) and BF Cartesian electron coordinates $\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i$. Its quantization requires \emph{no new postulate}: the internal nuclear configuration space is the flat Euclidean space of mass-weighted Cartesian displacements, and $(\Omega,Q)$ is merely a curvilinear chart of it (\S\,4.1). The exact kinetic operator is therefore the ordinary flat-space Laplacian, and everything else is calculus, carried out \emph{entirely in coordinate form}: we construct the metric of the chart, its determinant (which fixes the volume element) and its inverse (\S\S\,4.2--4.6), expand the Laplacian block by block writing out the explicit derivatives $\partial_\phi,\partial_\theta,\partial_\chi,\partial_{Q_b}$ at every stage (\S\S\,4.7--4.8), and finally reweight the wavefunction to the Watson measure, which turns the operator into Podolsky's manifestly Hermitian flat-measure form~\cite{podolsky1928} (\S\,4.9). Hermiticity is automatic at every step --- the Podolsky form is a symmetric sandwich of operators individually Hermitian on the flat measure --- and \emph{no commutator algebra of body-fixed angular momenta is ever used} (see the Remark in \S\,4.11). The only residue of the ordering is a single multiplicative function of the shape --- the Watson pseudopotential. The term-by-term expansion of every block, with each derivative written out, is collected in Appendix~B.

\subsection{No quantization postulate: the internal configuration space is flat}

Label the curvilinear configuration-space coordinates
\begin{equation}
\{q^{A}\}\;=\;\bigl\{\,\phi,\theta,\chi,\;Q_1,\dots,Q_f\,\bigr\},\qquad f\equiv 3N_{\mathrm{nucl}}-6, \tag{4.1}
\end{equation}
supplemented by the BF Cartesian electron coordinates $\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i$ (treated in \S\,4.4). The central observation of this section is that the coordinates (4.1) parametrize a \emph{flat} space. By (3.1), the mass-weighted nuclear displacements
\begin{equation*}
\boldsymbol\xi_j\equiv\sqrt{M_j}\,\mathbf R'_j=\mathbf S(\Omega)\,\mathbf u_j(Q),
\qquad
\mathbf u_j(Q)\equiv\sqrt{M_j}\,\bar{\mathbf R}_j(Q)=\mathbf e_j+\sum_b\mathbf l_{jb}\,Q_b,
\qquad
\mathbf e_j\equiv\sqrt{M_j}\,\bar{\mathbf R}^{\mathrm{eq}}_j,
\end{equation*}
lie, by the center-of-mass constraint $\sum_j\sqrt{M_j}\,\boldsymbol\xi_j=0$ [(2.4)], in a fixed linear subspace $\mathcal V\cong\mathbb R^{3N_{\mathrm{nucl}}-3}$ of the mass-weighted Cartesian space, on which the internal nuclear kinetic energy is the \emph{flat} quadratic form $T=\tfrac12\sum_j|\dot{\boldsymbol\xi}_j|^{2}$ [\S\,3.4]. The chart $(\Omega,Q)\mapsto\boldsymbol\xi$ has exactly $3+f=3N_{\mathrm{nucl}}-3$ coordinates, and the Eckart conditions (3.1b) make it locally invertible (away from the poles $\sin\theta=0$ and degenerate shapes). Consequently there is nothing to postulate: \emph{the exact quantum kinetic operator of the internal nuclear motion is the ordinary flat-space Laplacian} $\hat T_{\mathrm{rv}}=-\tfrac{\hbar^{2}}{2}\Delta_{\mathcal V}$, exactly as in Cartesian quantum mechanics, and the entire task of this section is calculus: express $\Delta_{\mathcal V}$ in the chart, and reweight the wavefunction to the Watson measure.

In classical mechanics, any kinetic energy that is quadratic in the velocities defines a \textbf{metric tensor} $g_{AB}(q)$ on configuration space,
\begin{equation}
T=\tfrac12\,g_{AB}(q)\,\dot q^{A}\dot q^{B},\qquad
p_A\equiv\frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot q^{A}}=g_{AB}\,\dot q^{B}, \tag{4.2}
\end{equation}
(repeated indices summed), and eliminating the velocities in favor of the momenta gives the Hamiltonian form
\begin{equation}
T=\tfrac12\,g^{AB}(q)\,p_A p_B ,\qquad g^{AB}\equiv (g^{-1})_{AB}. \tag{4.3}
\end{equation}
So three objects control everything: the metric $g_{AB}$, its inverse $g^{AB}$, and its determinant $g\equiv\det g_{AB}$. For our chart they are concrete geometry: $g_{AB}=\sum_j(\partial\boldsymbol\xi_j/\partial q^A)\!\cdot\!(\partial\boldsymbol\xi_j/\partial q^B)$ is the Gram matrix of the chart's tangent vectors --- the flat metric of $\mathcal V$ pulled back to the coordinates --- and $\sqrt g=|\det(\partial\boldsymbol\xi/\partial q)|$ is the Jacobian of the chart. We will build all three objects explicitly for the coordinates (4.1) in \S\,4.2--4.6.

Why can we not simply replace $p_A\to-i\hbar\,\partial_A$ in (4.3)? Because on a curved configuration space the physically meaningful inner product carries the Riemannian volume weight, $\langle\varphi|\psi\rangle=\int\varphi^{*}\psi\,\sqrt{g}\,d^{n}q$, and $-i\hbar\partial_A$ is \emph{not} Hermitian with respect to it. The one-dimensional case shows the problem completely: on $L^{2}(w(q)\,dq)$ with a positive weight $w$, integration by parts (boundary terms vanishing) gives
\begin{equation}
\int\varphi^{*}\bigl(-i\hbar\,\partial_q\psi\bigr)\,w\,dq
=\int\bigl(-i\hbar\,\partial_q\varphi\bigr)^{*}\psi\,w\,dq
\;-\;i\hbar\int\varphi^{*}\psi\;\frac{w'}{w}\,w\,dq ,
\tag{4.4}
\end{equation}
so the adjoint is $(-i\hbar\partial_q)^{\dagger}=-i\hbar\bigl(\partial_q+w'/w\bigr)\neq-i\hbar\partial_q$ whenever the weight is not constant. A kinetic operator built by na\"ive substitution would fail to be Hermitian and could produce complex expectation values. The same computation read backwards is the cure: the reweighting $\psi\mapsto w^{1/2}\psi$ maps $L^{2}(w\,dq)$ unitarily onto $L^{2}(dq)$, at the price of conjugating every operator by $w^{1/2}$ --- the mechanism we will use in \S\,4.9.

What, then, \emph{is} the flat Laplacian expressed in the chart? For any curvilinear chart of a flat space it takes the classical form
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\hat T \;=\; -\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2}\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{g}}\,\partial_A\!\bigl(\sqrt{g}\,g^{AB}\,\partial_B\bigr),\qquad g=\det g_{AB}.\;} \tag{4.5}
\end{equation}
To derive it, take $\Psi(\boldsymbol\xi)=\psi(q(\boldsymbol\xi))$ and $\Phi$ likewise. The chain rule gives $\nabla\Psi=\mathsf J^{-\mathsf T}\partial\psi$ with $\mathsf J=\partial\boldsymbol\xi/\partial q$, hence $\nabla\Phi^{*}\!\cdot\!\nabla\Psi=(\partial_A\varphi)^{*}g^{AB}(\partial_B\psi)$ and $d\boldsymbol\xi=\sqrt g\,d^nq$; the flat-space identity $\int\Phi^{*}(-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2}\Delta\Psi)\,d\boldsymbol\xi=\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2}\int\nabla\Phi^{*}\!\cdot\!\nabla\Psi\,d\boldsymbol\xi$ therefore reads, after undoing the integration by parts in the chart (boundary terms vanishing: periodicity in $\phi,\chi$, the vanishing of $\sin\theta$ at the poles $\theta=0,\pi$, and decay in $Q_b$),
\begin{equation}
\int\varphi^{*}\,\hat T\psi\;\sqrt{g}\,d^{n}q
=\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2}\int (\partial_A\varphi)^{*}\,g^{AB}\,(\partial_B\psi)\;\sqrt{g}\,d^{n}q , \tag{4.6}
\end{equation}
identifying (4.5) as the coordinate form of $-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2}\Delta_{\mathcal V}$. The right-hand side of (4.6) is manifestly symmetric under $\varphi\leftrightarrow\psi$ and positive for $\varphi=\psi$, so (4.5) is Hermitian on $L^{2}(\sqrt g\,d^nq)$ --- not by prescription, but because it \emph{is} the flat Laplacian, inheriting the Hermiticity of $-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2}\nabla^{2}$. Equation (4.5) is the same classical formula that yields the spherical-coordinates Laplacian; nothing quantum-mechanically new has been introduced. Our entire task in this section is therefore: \emph{compute $g_{AB}$, $g$, and $g^{AB}$ for the coordinates (4.1), expand (4.5), and reweight to the Watson measure (\S\,4.9)}.

\subsection{Body-frame kinematics: the Euler-angle derivatives, explicitly}

Fix the body-fixed orientation by the $zyz$ convention
\begin{equation}
\mathbf S(\phi,\theta,\chi)=R_z(\phi)\,R_y(\theta)\,R_z(\chi),\qquad
R_z(\psi)=\!\begin{pmatrix}\cos\psi&-\sin\psi&0\\ \sin\psi&\cos\psi&0\\ 0&0&1\end{pmatrix},\;
R_y(\vartheta)=\!\begin{pmatrix}\cos\vartheta&0&\sin\vartheta\\ 0&1&0\\ -\sin\vartheta&0&\cos\vartheta\end{pmatrix}, \tag{4.7}
\end{equation}
so that $\mathbf R'_j=\mathbf S\bar{\mathbf R}_j$ as in (3.1). The body-fixed angular velocity is defined by $(\mathbf S^{T}\dot{\mathbf S})_{\alpha\beta}=-\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}\omega_\gamma$ [eq.~(3.2)]; our first job is to express $\boldsymbol\omega$ through $(\dot\phi,\dot\theta,\dot\chi)$. Differentiating the one-parameter rotations at zero angle defines the generators
\begin{equation}
G_z\equiv\partial_\psi R_z\big|_{0}=\begin{pmatrix}0&-1&0\\1&0&0\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix},\qquad
G_y\equiv\partial_\vartheta R_y\big|_{0}=\begin{pmatrix}0&0&1\\0&0&0\\-1&0&0\end{pmatrix}, \tag{4.8}
\end{equation}
with $\partial_\psi R_z(\psi)=G_zR_z(\psi)$ and $\partial_\vartheta R_y(\vartheta)=G_yR_y(\vartheta)$ (check by direct differentiation of (4.7)). The chain rule applied to (4.7) then gives $\dot{\mathbf S}=\dot\phi\,G_z\mathbf S+\dot\theta\,R_z(\phi)G_yR_y(\theta)R_z(\chi)+\dot\chi\,\mathbf S G_z$, and multiplying by $\mathbf S^{T}$ from the left,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf S^{T}\dot{\mathbf S}
=\dot\phi\,\mathbf S^{T}G_z\mathbf S
+\dot\theta\,R_z(\chi)^{T}G_yR_z(\chi)
+\dot\chi\,G_z
=\bigl[\dot\phi\,\mathbf S^{T}\hat{\mathbf z}+\dot\theta\,R_z(\chi)^{T}\hat{\mathbf y}+\dot\chi\,\hat{\mathbf z}\bigr]_\times , \tag{4.9}
\end{equation}
where $[\mathbf a]_\times$ denotes the antisymmetric matrix with $([\mathbf a]_\times)_{\alpha\beta}=-\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}a_\gamma$, and we used the conjugation rule $R^{T}[\mathbf a]_\times R=[R^{T}\mathbf a]_\times$ for any rotation $R$, together with $R_y(\theta)^{T}\hat{\mathbf y}=\hat{\mathbf y}$. Evaluating the two nontrivial vectors from (4.7), $\mathbf S^{T}\hat{\mathbf z}=(-\sin\theta\cos\chi,\ \sin\theta\sin\chi,\ \cos\theta)^{T}$ and $R_z(\chi)^{T}\hat{\mathbf y}=(\sin\chi,\ \cos\chi,\ 0)^{T}$, and comparing with the definition of $\boldsymbol\omega$, we obtain $\boldsymbol\omega=\mathsf E(\Omega)\,\dot{\boldsymbol\Omega}$ with $\dot{\boldsymbol\Omega}=(\dot\phi,\dot\theta,\dot\chi)^{T}$ and the \textbf{kinematic matrix}
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\mathsf E(\Omega)=
\begin{pmatrix}
-\sin\theta\cos\chi & \sin\chi & 0\\[2pt]
\ \ \sin\theta\sin\chi & \cos\chi & 0\\[2pt]
\ \ \cos\theta & 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix},\qquad
\begin{aligned}
\omega_1&=-\dot\phi\,\sin\theta\cos\chi+\dot\theta\,\sin\chi,\\
\omega_2&=\ \ \dot\phi\,\sin\theta\sin\chi+\dot\theta\,\cos\chi,\\
\omega_3&=\ \ \dot\phi\,\cos\theta+\dot\chi .
\end{aligned}\;}\tag{4.10}
\end{equation}
Expanding the determinant along the third column,
\begin{equation}
\det\mathsf E
=1\cdot\det\!\begin{pmatrix}-\sin\theta\cos\chi&\sin\chi\\ \sin\theta\sin\chi&\cos\chi\end{pmatrix}
=-\sin\theta\,(\cos^2\chi+\sin^2\chi)=-\sin\theta,\qquad |\det\mathsf E|=\sin\theta . \tag{4.11}
\end{equation}
Solving the linear system (4.10) for the Euler rates --- multiply the $\omega_1,\omega_2$ lines by $(-\cos\chi,\sin\chi)$ and by $(\sin\chi,\cos\chi)$ respectively and add, then substitute into the $\omega_3$ line --- gives $\dot{\boldsymbol\Omega}=\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\omega$ with
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\mathsf E^{-1}(\Omega)=
\begin{pmatrix}
-\dfrac{\cos\chi}{\sin\theta} & \dfrac{\sin\chi}{\sin\theta} & 0\\[10pt]
\ \ \sin\chi & \cos\chi & 0\\[6pt]
\ \ \dfrac{\cos\theta\cos\chi}{\sin\theta} & -\dfrac{\cos\theta\sin\chi}{\sin\theta} & 1
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\begin{aligned}
\dot\phi&=\tfrac{1}{\sin\theta}\bigl(-\cos\chi\,\omega_1+\sin\chi\,\omega_2\bigr),\\
\dot\theta&=\sin\chi\,\omega_1+\cos\chi\,\omega_2,\\
\dot\chi&=\omega_3+\tfrac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}\bigl(\cos\chi\,\omega_1-\sin\chi\,\omega_2\bigr).
\end{aligned}\;}\tag{4.12}
\end{equation}
The $1/\sin\theta$ entries reflect the coordinate singularity of the Euler parametrization at the poles $\theta=0,\pi$ (where $\phi$ and $\chi$ describe the same rotation); it is compensated by the vanishing of $|\det\mathsf E|=\sin\theta$ in the volume element, and no physical quantity is singular there. Equations (4.10)--(4.12) are the only facts about the Euler angles used in the rest of this section.

\subsection{The ro-vibrational metric tensor}

The classical rotation--vibration kinetic energy in the body frame is the nuclear term of the Routhian (3.13),
\begin{equation}
2T_{\mathrm{rv}}
=\boldsymbol\omega^{T}\mathbf I_N(Q)\,\boldsymbol\omega
+2\,\boldsymbol\omega^{T}\!\sum_b\boldsymbol\sigma_b(Q)\,\dot Q_b
+\sum_b\dot Q_b^{\,2}, \tag{4.13}
\end{equation}
whose three pieces were derived in \S\,3.4: the nuclear inertia tensor $\mathbf I_N=\sum_jM_j(\bar R_j^2\mathbb 1-\bar{\mathbf R}_j\bar{\mathbf R}_j^{T})$ [(3.9)], the Coriolis vectors $\boldsymbol\sigma_b=\sum_a\boldsymbol\zeta_{ab}Q_a$ with \emph{constant} coefficients $\boldsymbol\zeta_{ab}=\sum_j\mathbf l_{ja}\times\mathbf l_{jb}$ [(3.17a)], and the identity vibrational metric $\sum_jM_j(\partial\bar{\mathbf R}_j/\partial Q_a)\!\cdot\!(\partial\bar{\mathbf R}_j/\partial Q_b)=\delta_{ab}$, a consequence of the mass-weighted rectilinear normal coordinates [(3.17)]. Collect the Coriolis vectors into the $3\times f$ matrix $\sigma_{\alpha b}\equiv(\boldsymbol\sigma_b)_\alpha$ and write (4.13) as one quadratic form in the \emph{physical} velocities,
\begin{equation}
2T_{\mathrm{rv}}=\begin{pmatrix}\boldsymbol\omega\\ \dot{\mathbf Q}\end{pmatrix}^{\!T}
\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf I_N & \boldsymbol\sigma\\ \boldsymbol\sigma^{T} & \mathbb 1_f\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}\boldsymbol\omega\\ \dot{\mathbf Q}\end{pmatrix}. \tag{4.14}
\end{equation}
The metric of (4.2), however, must be expressed in the \emph{coordinate} velocities $\dot q=(\dot\phi,\dot\theta,\dot\chi,\dot Q_b)$. The two are related linearly by (4.10): $\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}\boldsymbol\omega\\ \dot{\mathbf Q}\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)=\mathsf A\,\dot q$ with $\mathsf A=\mathrm{diag}(\mathsf E,\mathbb 1_f)$. Substituting into (4.14), the metric $g_{AB}$ defined by $2T_{\mathrm{rv}}=g_{AB}\dot q^A\dot q^B$ is $g=\mathsf A^{T}(\cdots)\mathsf A$:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
g_{AB}=
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I_N\,\mathsf E & \mathsf E^{T}\boldsymbol\sigma\\[3pt]
\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\mathsf E & \mathbb 1_f
\end{pmatrix},\qquad
\begin{aligned}
&\text{Euler--Euler block: }\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I_N\mathsf E\quad(3\times3),\\
&\text{Euler--mode block: }\mathsf E^{T}\boldsymbol\sigma\quad(3\times f),\\
&\text{mode--mode block: }\mathbb 1_f .
\end{aligned}\;}\tag{4.15}
\end{equation}
Every entry is now an explicit function of $(\theta,\chi,Q)$: the inertia tensor pulled back through the kinematic matrix, the Coriolis coupling pulled back through the same matrix, and a flat vibrational block. This single object encodes all four kinetic structures of the classical Hamiltonian (3.22) --- the rotational kernel, the Coriolis coupling, the vibrational kinetic energy, and the internal $\boldsymbol\pi^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\pi$ term --- as we verify in \S\,4.7 after inverting it.

\subsection{The electron sector: flat, constant, and decoupled from the measure}

Before expanding the Laplacian over (4.15) we must account for the electrons, since the kinetic operator involves the metric of the \emph{full} configuration space. Include the electron velocities: expanding the body-fixed electronic and mass-polarization kinetic terms via (3.9e), the complete velocity-form kinetic energy is
\begin{equation}
2T = \mathbf{v}^{\mathsf T}\mathsf{M}\,\mathbf{v},\qquad
\mathbf v=(\boldsymbol\omega,\dot{\mathbf Q},\dot{\bar{\mathbf r}}_i),\qquad
\mathsf{M}=\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{I} & \boldsymbol\sigma & \mathsf{C}\\[2pt] \boldsymbol\sigma^{\mathsf T} & \mathbb{1}_f & 0\\[2pt] \mathsf{C}^{\mathsf T} & 0 & \mathsf{D}\end{pmatrix},
\tag{4.16}
\end{equation}
with $\mathbf{I}=\mathbf{I}_N+\mathbf{I}_e-\mathbf{I}_X$ the full instantaneous inertia, $\mathsf{C}$ the rotation--electron coupling block [from $\boldsymbol\omega\!\cdot\!(\mathbf{L}_e-\mathbf{L}_X)$ in (3.9e)], and $\mathsf{D}_{(i\alpha)(j\beta)}=(m_e\delta_{ij}-m_e^2/M_{\mathrm{tot}})\,\delta_{\alpha\beta}$ the \emph{constant} electron mass matrix. Eliminating the electron block by its Schur complement --- i.e.\ completing the square in $\dot{\bar{\mathbf r}}_i$ --- removes all $\boldsymbol\omega$-dependence from the electron sector: the correction is precisely the electronic-plus-mass-polarization inertia, $\mathsf{C}\mathsf{D}^{-1}\mathsf{C}^{\mathsf T}=\mathbf{I}_e-\mathbf{I}_X$, so $\mathbf{I}-\mathsf{C}\mathsf{D}^{-1}\mathsf{C}^{\mathsf T}=\mathbf{I}_N$ --- the same exact cancellation proven classically in the cross-check of \S\,3.6 [eq.~(3.18)]. Hence
\begin{equation}
\det\mathsf{M}
=\det\mathsf{D}\;\det\!\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{I}_N & \boldsymbol\sigma\\ \boldsymbol\sigma^{\mathsf T} & \mathbb 1_f\end{pmatrix},
\qquad \det\mathsf D=\text{const}, \tag{4.17}
\end{equation}
and the surviving determinant is exactly that of the ro-vibrational block (4.14). Two consequences. \emph{(i)} The electron coordinates contribute only a constant factor to the volume element --- their measure is the flat $\prod_id^3\bar{\mathbf r}_i$, and $\hat{\bar{\mathbf p}}_i=-i\hbar\nabla_{\bar{\mathbf r}_i}$ is Hermitian on it with no weight corrections. \emph{(ii)} In the canonical (momentum) form (3.22), the electrons couple to rotation only through the shift $\mathbf J\to\mathbf J-\mathbf L_{\mathrm{elec}}$, and the operator $\hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\alpha}=-i\hbar\sum_i\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}\bar r_{i\beta}\partial_{\bar r_{i\gamma}}$ is Hermitian on the flat electron measure \emph{without any symmetrization}, because its vector field is divergence-free:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{i\gamma}\partial_{\bar r_{i\gamma}}\bigl(\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}\bar r_{i\beta}\bigr)
=\sum_i\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}\,\delta_{\beta\gamma}=0 . \tag{4.18}
\end{equation}
Consequently the entire quantization problem lives in the ro-vibrational sector: we expand the flat Laplacian over the ro-vibrational chart --- the metric (4.15) --- and the electrons enter the result only through the flat kinetic operator of \S\,4.10 and the uniform shift $\hat J_\alpha\to\hat J_\alpha-\hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\alpha}$, which preserves Hermiticity term by term because $\hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\alpha}$ is Hermitian and acts on a different set of coordinates than every ro-vibrational object (the term-by-term verification is Appendix~B).

\subsection{Determinant and the volume element}

We need $g=\det g_{AB}$ for the metric (4.15). Use the block-determinant identity: for any square blocks with invertible lower-right corner,
\begin{equation}
\det\!\begin{pmatrix}\mathsf a&\mathsf b\\ \mathsf b^{T}&\mathbb 1\end{pmatrix}
=\det\!\bigl(\mathsf a-\mathsf b\,\mathsf b^{T}\bigr), \tag{4.19}
\end{equation}
which follows by block row reduction --- subtracting $\mathsf b\times(\text{bottom row})$ from the top row produces the block-triangular matrix $\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}\mathsf a-\mathsf b\mathsf b^{T}&0\\ \mathsf b^{T}&\mathbb 1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, whose determinant is the product of the diagonal blocks. Applying (4.19) to (4.15) with $\mathsf a=\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I_N\mathsf E$, $\mathsf b=\mathsf E^{T}\boldsymbol\sigma$:
\begin{equation}
\det g_{AB}
=\det\!\bigl[\mathsf E^{T}(\mathbf I_N-\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T})\mathsf E\bigr]
=(\det\mathsf E)^2\,\det\mathbf I'
\;\Longrightarrow\;
\boxed{\;\det g_{AB}=\sin^2\!\theta\;\gamma(Q),\quad
\gamma\equiv\det\mathbf I',\quad
\mathbf I'\equiv\mathbf I_N-\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T},\;}\tag{4.20}
\end{equation}
using $\det(\mathsf E^{T}X\mathsf E)=(\det\mathsf E)^2\det X$ and (4.11). Remarkably, the determinant is governed by the \emph{Watson-modified} inertia $\mathbf I'=\mathbf I_N-\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}$ of (3.20) --- not the bare $\mathbf I_N$: the Coriolis coupling $\boldsymbol\sigma$ reduces the rotational determinant, and it is the same $\mathbf I'$ whose inverse $\boldsymbol\mu=(\mathbf I')^{-1}$ is the rotational kernel of (3.22). The Riemannian volume element is therefore
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\sqrt{g}\,d^{\,3+f}q=\underbrace{\sin\theta\,d\phi\,d\theta\,d\chi}_{\text{Haar measure on }SO(3)}\;\underbrace{\sqrt{\gamma(Q)}\,\textstyle\prod_b dQ_b}_{\text{rot.--vib.\ Jacobian}},\qquad \sqrt{g}=\sin\theta\,\sqrt{\gamma(Q)},\;}\tag{4.21}
\end{equation}
times the flat, constant electron factor $\prod_id^3\bar{\mathbf r}_i$ from \S\,4.4. The two curved factors have transparent origins: $\sin\theta$ is the Euler-angle Jacobian $|\det\mathsf E|$ of (4.11), and $\sqrt\gamma$ is the shape-dependent part carrying the Coriolis-reduced inertia. Indeed $\sqrt g=\sin\theta\sqrt\gamma$ is nothing but the Jacobian $|\det(\partial\boldsymbol\xi/\partial(\Omega,Q))|$ of the chart: (4.21) is the flat volume element $d\boldsymbol\xi$ written in ro-vibrational coordinates.

\subsection{The inverse metric}

The kinetic operator (4.5) needs $g^{AB}$. We claim
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
g^{AB}=
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\,\mathsf E^{-T} & -\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\,\boldsymbol\sigma\\[3pt]
-\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\,\mathsf E^{-T} & \mathbb 1_f+\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\,\boldsymbol\sigma
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad \boldsymbol\mu\equiv(\mathbf I')^{-1}=(\mathbf I_N-\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T})^{-1},\;}\tag{4.22}
\end{equation}
with $\mathsf E^{-T}\equiv(\mathsf E^{-1})^{T}$ from (4.12). Rather than derive it, \emph{verify} it --- multiply (4.15) by (4.22) block by block, using only $\boldsymbol\mu\mathbf I'=\mathbb 1$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{(top-left)}\quad &\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I_N\mathsf E\cdot\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\mathsf E^{-T}-\mathsf E^{T}\boldsymbol\sigma\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\mathsf E^{-T}
=\mathsf E^{T}(\mathbf I_N-\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T})\boldsymbol\mu\,\mathsf E^{-T}=\mathsf E^{T}\mathsf E^{-T}=\mathbb 1_3,\\
\text{(top-right)}\quad &-\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I_N\mathsf E\cdot\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma+\mathsf E^{T}\boldsymbol\sigma\,(\mathbb 1_f+\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma)
=\mathsf E^{T}\bigl[-(\mathbf I_N-\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T})\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma+\boldsymbol\sigma\bigr]=\mathsf E^{T}(-\boldsymbol\sigma+\boldsymbol\sigma)=0,\\
\text{(bottom-left)}\quad &\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\mathsf E\cdot\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\mathsf E^{-T}-\mathbb 1_f\cdot\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\mathsf E^{-T}=0,\\
\text{(bottom-right)}\quad &-\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\mathsf E\cdot\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma+\mathbb 1_f+\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma=\mathbb 1_f .
\end{aligned}\tag{4.23}
\end{equation}
So $g_{AB}\,g^{BC}=\delta_A^{\ C}$, confirming (4.22). Note how the Watson reciprocal inertia $\boldsymbol\mu$ --- introduced classically in (3.20) by inverting the momentum--velocity relation --- reappears here as the geometric inverse of the metric: the two inversions are the same computation.

\subsection{The rotational derivative operators (defined, not postulated)}

Expanding the Laplacian (4.5) will repeatedly produce the combination $(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\partial_{\Omega_s}$ (index $s$ running over $\phi,\theta,\chi$). It is convenient to give it a name:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\hat J_\alpha\equiv-i\hbar\sum_{s}(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\,\partial_{\Omega_s}:\qquad
\begin{aligned}
\hat J_1&=-i\hbar\Bigl[-\tfrac{\cos\chi}{\sin\theta}\,\partial_\phi+\sin\chi\,\partial_\theta+\tfrac{\cos\theta\cos\chi}{\sin\theta}\,\partial_\chi\Bigr],\\
\hat J_2&=-i\hbar\Bigl[\ \ \tfrac{\sin\chi}{\sin\theta}\,\partial_\phi+\cos\chi\,\partial_\theta-\tfrac{\cos\theta\sin\chi}{\sin\theta}\,\partial_\chi\Bigr],\\
\hat J_3&=-i\hbar\,\partial_\chi ,
\end{aligned}\;}\tag{4.24}
\end{equation}
reading the rows of $\mathsf E^{-1}$ off (4.12). We stress the logic: (4.24) is a \emph{definition} --- shorthand for three explicit combinations of Euler-angle derivatives --- and we will never compute a commutator $[\hat J_\alpha,\hat J_\beta]$ or use any angular-momentum algebra. The definition is natural because classically $J_\alpha=\partial T_{\mathrm{rv}}/\partial\omega_\alpha$ is conjugate to $\boldsymbol\omega$: from $\boldsymbol\omega=\mathsf E\dot{\boldsymbol\Omega}$ and the chain rule, $p_{\Omega_s}=\partial T/\partial\dot\Omega_s=(\partial\omega_\alpha/\partial\dot\Omega_s)\,J_\alpha=(\mathsf E)_{\alpha s}J_\alpha$, i.e.\ $p_\Omega=\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf J$ and $J_\alpha=(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\,p_{\Omega_s}$ --- (4.24) is its coordinate representation. As a consistency check, substituting $p_\Omega=\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf J$ and $P_b$ into $\tfrac12 p_A g^{AB}p_B$ with (4.22) collapses all $\mathsf E$ factors,
\begin{equation}
\tfrac12\,p_Ag^{AB}p_B
=\tfrac12\,\mathbf J^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\,\mathbf J-\mathbf J^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\,\boldsymbol\pi+\tfrac12\,\boldsymbol\pi^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\,\boldsymbol\pi+\tfrac12\sum_bP_b^2,
\qquad \boldsymbol\pi\equiv\boldsymbol\sigma P=\sum_b\boldsymbol\sigma_bP_b , \tag{4.25}
\end{equation}
which is exactly the nuclear kernel of (3.22) [the electronic shift $\mathbf J\to\mathbf J-\mathbf L_{\mathrm{elec}}$ enters per \S\,4.4]. So the classical correspondence is verified before any quantization.

Two coordinate identities make the operators (4.24) as good as Cartesian momenta. First, a \textbf{divergence identity}: the weighted divergence of each column of $\mathsf E^{-1}$ vanishes. Compute all three explicitly from (4.12):
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\alpha=1:\quad \sum_s\partial_{\Omega_s}\!\bigl(\sin\theta\,(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s1}\bigr)
&=\partial_\phi(-\cos\chi)+\partial_\theta(\sin\theta\sin\chi)+\partial_\chi(\cos\theta\cos\chi)\\
&=0+\cos\theta\sin\chi-\cos\theta\sin\chi=0,\\
\alpha=2:\quad \sum_s\partial_{\Omega_s}\!\bigl(\sin\theta\,(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s2}\bigr)
&=\partial_\phi(\sin\chi)+\partial_\theta(\sin\theta\cos\chi)+\partial_\chi(-\cos\theta\sin\chi)\\
&=0+\cos\theta\cos\chi-\cos\theta\cos\chi=0,\\
\alpha=3:\quad \sum_s\partial_{\Omega_s}\!\bigl(\sin\theta\,(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s3}\bigr)
&=\partial_\chi(\sin\theta)=0 .
\end{aligned}\tag{4.26}
\end{equation}
Second, this identity makes each $\hat J_\alpha$ \textbf{Hermitian on the Haar measure} $\sin\theta\,d\phi\,d\theta\,d\chi$: integrating by parts (periodicity in $\phi,\chi$; $\sin\theta\to0$ at the poles),
\begin{equation}
\int\varphi^{*}\,\hat J_\alpha\psi\,\sin\theta\,d\Omega
=\int(\hat J_\alpha\varphi)^{*}\psi\,\sin\theta\,d\Omega
+i\hbar\int\varphi^{*}\psi\,\Bigl[\underbrace{\textstyle\sum_s\partial_{\Omega_s}\!\bigl(\sin\theta(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\bigr)}_{=\,0\ \text{by (4.26)}}\Bigr]d\Omega , \tag{4.27}
\end{equation}
so $\hat J_\alpha^{\dagger}=\hat J_\alpha$ with \emph{no} symmetrization needed. In differential-geometric language, (4.26) says the vector fields generating body-frame rotations are divergence-free with respect to the Haar measure; it is the coordinate-level fact that replaces every appeal to angular-momentum algebra in what follows. [The same argument with the flat measure and (4.18) makes $\hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\alpha}$ Hermitian, and the vibrational analogue appears in \S\,4.9.]

\subsection{The kinetic operator, block by block}

Insert the measure (4.21) and the inverse metric (4.22) into the Laplacian (4.5) and split the double sum over $A,B$ into the four blocks:
\begin{equation}
\hat T_{\mathrm{rv}}
=\underbrace{-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2\sqrt g}\,\partial_{\Omega_s}\!\bigl(\sqrt g\,g^{\Omega\Omega}_{st}\partial_{\Omega_t}\bigr)}_{\hat T_{\mathrm{rot}}}
\;\underbrace{-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2\sqrt g}\Bigl[\partial_{\Omega_s}\!\bigl(\sqrt g\,g^{\Omega Q}_{sb}\partial_{Q_b}\bigr)+\partial_{Q_b}\!\bigl(\sqrt g\,g^{Q\Omega}_{bs}\partial_{\Omega_s}\bigr)\Bigr]}_{\hat T_{\mathrm{Cor}}}
\;\underbrace{-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2\sqrt g}\,\partial_{Q_b}\!\bigl(\sqrt g\,g^{QQ}_{bc}\partial_{Q_c}\bigr)}_{\hat T_{\mathrm{vib}}} . \tag{4.28}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Rotational block.}
In $\hat T_{\mathrm{rot}}$, the factors $\sqrt\gamma$, $\mu_{\alpha\beta}(Q)$ are independent of the Euler angles, so they pass through $\partial_{\Omega_s}$ and the $\sqrt\gamma$ cancels against the prefactor. Writing $g^{\Omega\Omega}_{st}=(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\mu_{\alpha\beta}(\mathsf E^{-1})_{t\beta}$ and expanding the outer derivative by the product rule,
\begin{align}
\hat T_{\mathrm{rot}}
&=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2}\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\Bigl[\underbrace{(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\,\partial_{\Omega_s}\bigl((\mathsf E^{-1})_{t\beta}\,\partial_{\Omega_t}\bigr)}_{\text{both derivatives survive}}
+\underbrace{\tfrac{1}{\sin\theta}\Bigl(\textstyle\sum_s\partial_{\Omega_s}\bigl(\sin\theta(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\bigr)\Bigr)}_{=\,0\ \text{by (4.26)}}(\mathsf E^{-1})_{t\beta}\,\partial_{\Omega_t}\Bigr]\nonumber\\[2pt]
&=\tfrac12\sum_{\alpha\beta}\mu_{\alpha\beta}(Q)\,\hat J_\alpha\hat J_\beta ,
\tag{4.29}
\end{align}
by the definition (4.24). The divergence identity (4.26) has done all the work: the rotational block is \emph{exactly} the symmetric kernel $\tfrac12\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat J_\alpha\hat J_\beta$, Hermitian as written [each $\hat J_\alpha$ is Hermitian by (4.27), $\mu_{\alpha\beta}$ is a real symmetric function of $Q$ commuting with both, and taking the adjoint merely reverses the order, $(\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat J_\alpha\hat J_\beta)^{\dagger}=\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat J_\beta\hat J_\alpha$, which upon relabeling the summed indices $\alpha\leftrightarrow\beta$ and using $\mu_{\beta\alpha}=\mu_{\alpha\beta}$ is the original operator], and it contributes \emph{no} extra potential term. In the traditional treatment this last fact is derived from the anomalous commutator algebra; here it is simply the statement that a rigid rotor has no pseudopotential, verified by the three explicit derivative computations (4.26).

\paragraph{Coriolis block.}
In $\hat T_{\mathrm{Cor}}$, the first group carries $\partial_{\Omega_s}$, which passes through the $Q$-functions $\mu,\boldsymbol\sigma,\sqrt\gamma$ and, by the same divergence identity (4.26), collapses onto $\hat J_\alpha$ acting from the left. The second group carries $\partial_{Q_b}$, which passes through $\mathsf E^{-1}(\Omega)$ and $\sin\theta$ but \emph{acts} on $\sqrt\gamma$, $\mu$, and $\boldsymbol\sigma$. The result (each step is one product rule; the full expansion is Appendix~B) is
\begin{equation}
\hat T_{\mathrm{Cor}}
=-\tfrac12\Bigl[\hat J_\alpha\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat\pi_\beta
+\tfrac{1}{\sqrt\gamma}\,\hat P_b\,\sqrt\gamma\;\sigma_{\alpha b}\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat J_\beta\Bigr],
\qquad
\hat P_b\equiv-i\hbar\,\partial_{Q_b},\quad
\hat\pi_\alpha\equiv\sum_b\sigma_{\alpha b}(Q)\,\hat P_b . \tag{4.30}
\end{equation}
The two groups are adjoints of each other on the measure (4.21) [the $\gamma^{\pm1/2}$ factors in the second group are precisely what integration by parts over $\sqrt\gamma\,dQ$ produces], so their sum is Hermitian --- again automatically, with no ordering imposed by hand.

\paragraph{Vibrational block.}
With $g^{QQ}_{bc}=\delta_{bc}+(\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma)_{bc}$ and the $\sin\theta$ cancelling,
\begin{equation}
\hat T_{\mathrm{vib}}
=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2}\,\frac{1}{\sqrt\gamma}\sum_{b,c}\partial_{Q_b}\!\Bigl(\sqrt\gamma\;\bigl[\delta_{bc}+(\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma)_{bc}\bigr]\,\partial_{Q_c}\Bigr). \tag{4.31}
\end{equation}
Expanding the $\delta_{bc}$ part by one product rule exhibits the problem announced in (4.4): $-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2}\gamma^{-1/2}\partial_{Q_b}(\gamma^{1/2}\partial_{Q_b}\psi)=\tfrac12\hat P_b^2\psi-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2}\bigl(\tfrac12\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma\bigr)\partial_{Q_b}\psi$ --- the bare $\hat P_b$ is not Hermitian on the curved measure $\sqrt\gamma\,dQ$, and the first-order remainder is the compensation. Removing these remainders once and for all is the job of the reweighting below.

\subsection{Reweighting to the Watson measure: Podolsky's form and the pseudopotential}

The wavefunction inherited from the flat space is normalized on $\sqrt g\,d^{\,3+f}q$. Watson's convention works instead with the measure $d\mu_W\equiv\sin\theta\,d\phi\,d\theta\,d\chi\,\prod_bdQ_b\,\prod_id^3\bar{\mathbf r}_i$: absorb the shape factor of the volume element into the wavefunction,
\begin{equation}
\psi_W\equiv\gamma^{1/4}\,\psi
\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad
|\psi|^{2}\,\sqrt g\;d^{\,3+f}q=|\psi_W|^{2}\,d\mu_W , \tag{4.32}
\end{equation}
so $\psi_W\in L^{2}(d\mu_W)$, and conjugate every operator: $\hat O_W=\gamma^{1/4}\,\hat O\,\gamma^{-1/4}$. Only the $Q$-sector is affected ($\gamma$ depends on $Q$ alone); the $\sin\theta$ stays in the measure, on which the $\hat J_\alpha$ are already Hermitian by (4.27), and the electron sector is flat. Conjugating the blocks (4.29)--(4.31) assembles the ro-vibrational operator into a single sandwich,
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\hat T_{\mathrm{rv},W}\equiv\gamma^{1/4}\,\hat T_{\mathrm{rv}}\,\gamma^{-1/4}
=\tfrac12\,\gamma^{-1/4}\Bigl[\hat J_\alpha\,\gamma^{1/2}\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat J_\beta
-\hat J_\alpha\,\gamma^{1/2}(\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma)_{\alpha b}\,\hat P_b
-\hat P_b\,\gamma^{1/2}(\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu)_{b\beta}\,\hat J_\beta
+\hat P_b\,\gamma^{1/2}g^{QQ}_{bc}\,\hat P_c\Bigr]\gamma^{-1/4},\;}\tag{4.32$'$}
\end{equation}
the equality being checked factor by factor against (4.29)--(4.31): the rotational block is untouched because $\gamma$ commutes with the $\hat J_\alpha$, and in the Coriolis and vibrational blocks the explicit powers of $\gamma$ combine identically. Equation (4.32$'$) is \textbf{Podolsky's form} of the kinetic operator~\cite{podolsky1928}, adapted to the Watson measure. Its virtue is that Hermiticity on $L^{2}(d\mu_W)$ is now \emph{manifest}, with no integration by parts: taking the adjoint merely reverses each product --- using only $\hat J_\alpha^{\dagger}=\hat J_\alpha$ (4.27), $\hat P_b^{\dagger}=\hat P_b$, the reality of $\gamma$, and the symmetry of the coefficient blocks --- and returns the same expression. All that remains is to \emph{order} the sandwich: push the $\gamma^{\pm1/4}$ factors through the derivatives and collect the multiplicative residue. Ordering the identity part of the vibrational block, working from the innermost derivative outward (each line is one product rule):
\begin{align}
\partial_{Q_b}\!\bigl(\gamma^{-1/4}\psi_W\bigr)&=\gamma^{-1/4}\Bigl[\partial_{Q_b}\psi_W-\tfrac14(\partial_{Q_b}\!\ln\gamma)\,\psi_W\Bigr],\nonumber\\
\gamma^{1/2}\,\partial_{Q_b}\!\bigl(\gamma^{-1/4}\psi_W\bigr)&=\gamma^{1/4}\Bigl[\partial_{Q_b}\psi_W-\tfrac14(\partial_{Q_b}\!\ln\gamma)\,\psi_W\Bigr],\nonumber\\
\partial_{Q_b}\Bigl(\gamma^{1/4}\bigl[\,\cdots\,\bigr]\Bigr)&=\gamma^{1/4}\Bigl[\partial_{Q_b}^{2}\psi_W-\tfrac{1}{16}(\partial_{Q_b}\!\ln\gamma)^{2}\,\psi_W-\tfrac14(\partial_{Q_b}^{2}\ln\gamma)\,\psi_W\Bigr], \tag{4.33}
\end{align}
where in the last line the two cross terms $\pm\tfrac14(\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma)\,\partial_{Q_b}\psi_W$ (one from differentiating $\gamma^{1/4}$, one from the bracket) cancel. Multiplying by $-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2}\gamma^{1/4}\cdot\gamma^{-1/2}=-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2}\gamma^{-1/4}$ and summing over $b$: the identity part becomes the bare $\tfrac12\sum_b\hat P_b^2$ plus a pure multiplicative function,
\begin{equation}
U_{\mathrm{vib}}=\frac{\hbar^{2}}{8}\sum_b\Bigl[\partial_{Q_b}^{2}\ln\gamma+\tfrac14\bigl(\partial_{Q_b}\!\ln\gamma\bigr)^{2}\Bigr]. \tag{4.34}
\end{equation}
The Coriolis part of the sandwich orders even more simply: in its first group, $\gamma^{1/4}\hat P_b\gamma^{-1/4}=\hat P_b+\tfrac{i\hbar}{4}(\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma)$; in the second, $\gamma^{-1/4}\hat P_b\gamma^{1/4}=\hat P_b-\tfrac{i\hbar}{4}(\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma)$. The two induced terms are equal and opposite [they multiply the same function, since $\hat J_\alpha\mu_{\alpha\beta}\sigma_{\beta b}=\sigma_{\beta b}\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat J_\alpha$ upon relabeling the summed Cartesian indices --- these are functions of different variables and commute], so they cancel, leaving the manifestly symmetric flat-measure form
\begin{equation}
\hat T_{\mathrm{Cor},W}=-\tfrac12\sum_{\alpha\beta}\hat J_\alpha\bigl(\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\hat\pi_\beta+\hat\pi_\beta\,\mu_{\alpha\beta}\bigr)
\;=\;-\tfrac12\bigl(\hat J_\alpha\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat\pi_\beta+\hat\pi_\alpha\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat J_\beta\bigr), \tag{4.35}
\end{equation}
with \emph{no} extra potential from this block. Finally, ordering the $\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma$-dressed part of the vibrational block and reorganizing it into the symmetric kernel $\tfrac12\hat\pi_\alpha\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat\pi_\beta$ leaves a second multiplicative residue, $U_{\mathrm{Cor}}$ (every derivative of this bookkeeping is written out in Appendix~B).

The total extra potential $\hat U=U_{\mathrm{vib}}+U_{\mathrm{Cor}}$ collapses through two exact identities, both pure $Q$-calculus. First, Jacobi's formula --- for a symmetric positive-definite matrix, $\ln\det\mathbf I'=\operatorname{tr}\ln\mathbf I'$, so differentiating,
\begin{equation}
\partial_{Q_b}\!\ln\gamma=\operatorname{tr}\!\bigl(\boldsymbol\mu\,\partial_{Q_b}\mathbf I'\bigr),
\qquad
\partial_{Q_b}\boldsymbol\mu=-\,\boldsymbol\mu\,(\partial_{Q_b}\mathbf I')\,\boldsymbol\mu
\quad[\text{from }\partial_{Q_b}(\boldsymbol\mu\,\mathbf I')=0], \tag{4.36}
\end{equation}
expresses every derivative of $\gamma$ and $\boldsymbol\mu$ through $\partial_{Q_b}\mathbf I'$. Second, the Eckart conditions endow $\mathbf I'$ with a remarkable exact closed form, due to Watson: with the \emph{constant} equilibrium inertia derivatives $a^{b}\equiv(\partial\mathbf I_N/\partial Q_b)|_{Q=0}$,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf I'(Q)=A(Q)\,(\mathbf I^{e})^{-1}A(Q),\qquad
A(Q)\equiv\mathbf I^{e}+\tfrac12\sum_b a^{b}\,Q_b,\qquad
\mathbf I^{e}\equiv\mathbf I_N(0), \tag{4.37}
\end{equation}
proven term by term from the Eckart completeness relations in Appendix~B (\S\,B.6). Hence $\gamma=(\det A)^2/\det\mathbf I^{e}$ and $\boldsymbol\mu=A^{-1}\mathbf I^{e}A^{-1}$: every derivative of $\gamma$ and $\boldsymbol\mu$ reduces to the constant matrices $a^{b}$. Substituting (4.36)--(4.37) into $U_{\mathrm{vib}}+U_{\mathrm{Cor}}$ and collapsing the sums with the Eckart sum rules --- the complete term-by-term algebra is \S\,B.6 --- the outcome, first obtained by Watson~\cite{watson1968}, is the single trace
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\hat U(Q)\;=\;-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{8}\sum_{\alpha=1}^{3}\mu_{\alpha\alpha}(Q),\qquad \boldsymbol{\mu}=(\mathbf{I}_N-\boldsymbol{\sigma}\boldsymbol{\sigma}^{\mathsf T})^{-1}.\;} \tag{4.38}
\end{equation}
Two remarks. \emph{(a)} The collapse is special to \emph{rectilinear} normal coordinates: for general curvilinear internal coordinates the closed form (4.37) fails and additional extra-potential terms survive. \emph{(b)} The electrons do not modify $\hat U$: $\gamma$ and $\boldsymbol\mu$ depend on $Q$ alone, and the electronic Jacobian is flat (\S\,4.4). The pseudopotential is a $c$-number on the nuclear shape space, fixed entirely by the modified inertia $\mathbf I'$.

\subsection{Electronic kinetic operator}

In BF Cartesian electron coordinates, $\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i = -i\hbar\nabla_{\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i}$ is Hermitian on $L^{2}(\mathbb{R}^{3N_{\mathrm{elec}}},\prod_id^{3}\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i)$ (\S\,4.4). The electronic + mass-polarization kinetic operator is therefore the straightforward quantization of (3.21):
\begin{equation}
\hat T_e \;=\; \sum_i\frac{\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i^{\,2}}{2m_e} \;+\; \frac{1}{2M_N}\Bigl(\sum_i\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i\Bigr)^{\!2}. \tag{4.39}
\end{equation}
Expanding the second piece:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{2M_N}\Bigl(\sum_i\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i\Bigr)^{\!2} \;=\; \sum_i\frac{\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i^{\,2}}{2M_N} \;+\; \frac{1}{M_N}\!\sum_{i<l}\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i\!\cdot\!\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_l. \tag{4.40}
\end{equation}
The diagonal $1/(2M_N)$ piece renormalizes the effective electron mass through $1/m_e + 1/M_N = M_{\mathrm{tot}}/(m_e M_N) = 1/\mu_e$. The off-diagonal two-electron term is the mass-polarization \emph{sensu stricto}, dynamically coupling electrons through nuclear recoil --- a purely classical effect (Section 2.4) inherited by the quantum theory.

\subsection{Complete quantum molecular Hamiltonian}

Collect the flat-measure blocks: the rotational kernel (4.29), the Coriolis coupling (4.35), the vibrational operator [bare $\tfrac12\sum_b\hat P_b^2$ plus the symmetric $\tfrac12\hat\pi_\alpha\mu_{\alpha\beta}\hat\pi_\beta$], the pseudopotential (4.38), the electronic operator (4.39), the shift $\hat J_\alpha\to\hat J_\alpha-\hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\alpha}$ of \S\,4.4, and the rotationally invariant Coulomb potentials of (3.8). The pieces assemble into the sandwich-ordered kernel [the term-by-term identification of the nine products with the blocks above, including every derivative, is Appendix~B]:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
\hat H_{\mathrm{int}}\;=\;& \tfrac12\sum_{\alpha\beta}\bigl(\hat J_\alpha - \hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\alpha} - \hat\pi_\alpha\bigr)\mu_{\alpha\beta}(Q)\bigl(\hat J_\beta - \hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\beta} - \hat\pi_\beta\bigr) \\
& + \tfrac12\sum_b\hat P_b^2 \\
& + \sum_i\frac{\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i^{\,2}}{2m_e} \;+\; \frac{1}{2M_N}\Bigl(\sum_i\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i\Bigr)^{\!2} \\
& - \frac{\hbar^{2}}{8}\sum_{\alpha}\mu_{\alpha\alpha}(Q) \\
& + V_{NN}(Q) \;+\; V_{Ne}(Q,\{\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i\}) \;+\; V_{ee}(\{\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i\}),
\end{aligned}}\tag{4.41}
\end{equation}
acting on $L^{2}(\mathcal{C},\,d\mu_W)$ with $d\mu_W = \sin\theta\,d\phi\, d\theta\, d\chi\,\prod_b dQ_b\,\prod_i d^{3}\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i$. Every operator in (4.41) is an explicit differential operator in the coordinates --- the $\hat J_\alpha$ are the Euler-angle combinations (4.24), $\hat\pi_\alpha=\sum_b\sigma_{\alpha b}\hat P_b$, $\hat P_b=-i\hbar\partial_{Q_b}$, $\hat{\bar{\mathbf p}}_i=-i\hbar\nabla_{\bar{\mathbf r}_i}$ --- and every one is Hermitian on $d\mu_W$ by the integration-by-parts arguments given above; indeed (4.41) is exactly the ordered form of the Podolsky sandwich (4.32$'$).

This is the \textbf{exact non-relativistic molecular Hamiltonian} after separation of the total COM motion, in BF + Eckart frame, with electrons referred to the nuclear COM. The only approximations made along the way were: (i) non-relativistic dynamics; (ii) Eckart's choice of the BF orientation (a convention, not an approximation); (iii) parametrization of the nuclear shape by $3N_{\mathrm{nucl}}-6$ internal coordinates $Q_b$ (a parametrization, also exact).

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Remark (the anomalous commutator, not needed).} The traditional route to (4.41)~\cite{klein1929,vanvleck1951,bunkerjensen} defines the body-fixed angular momentum by projecting the space-fixed operator through the direction cosines, derives its \emph{anomalous} commutation relations $[\hat J_\alpha,\hat J_\beta]=-i\hbar\,\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}\hat J_\gamma$, and uses that algebra to control the operator ordering of the rotational kernel. Nothing in this section used it: the $\hat J_\alpha$ entered only as the shorthand (4.24) for explicit Euler-angle derivatives, Hermiticity came from the divergence identity (4.26) and the manifest sandwich structure of Podolsky's form (4.32$'$), and the pseudopotential came from the $Q$-derivatives of the volume element. The anomalous algebra is a correct and sometimes useful property of the operators (4.24), but it is a \emph{consequence} of the coordinate representation, not an input to the quantization.

\subsection{Born--Huang expansion}

\subsubsection{Clamped-nuclei electronic Hamiltonian}

At each nuclear configuration $Q$, define the \textbf{BF clamped-nuclei electronic Hamiltonian} as the sum of all $Q$-parametric electron-only pieces of (4.41):
\begin{equation}
\hat H_{\mathrm{el}}(Q) \;\equiv\; \sum_i\frac{\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i^{\,2}}{2m_e} \;+\; \frac{1}{2M_N}\Bigl(\sum_i\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i\Bigr)^{\!2} \;+\; V_{Ne}(Q,\{\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i\}) \;+\; V_{ee}(\{\bar{\mathbf{r}}_i\}). \tag{4.42}
\end{equation}
\emph{Remark.} The mass-polarization piece $(\sum_i\hat{\bar{\mathbf{p}}}_i)^{2}/(2M_N)$ is included here as a small correction with $1/M_N$. Some treatments (``clamped-nuclei BO'') drop it; we keep it explicit because it is parametric in $Q$ only through the absent dependence (i.e., $M_N$ is just a number), which means it acts trivially on the parametric family of electronic Hilbert spaces.

Solve the parametric eigenvalue problem:
\begin{equation}
\hat H_{\mathrm{el}}(Q)\,|\phi_n(Q)\rangle \;=\; E_n^{\mathrm{el}}(Q)\,|\phi_n(Q)\rangle, \tag{4.43}
\end{equation}
in the electronic Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}_e = L^{2}(\mathbb{R}^{3N_{\mathrm{elec}}},d^{3N_{\mathrm{elec}}}\bar{\mathbf{r}})$. The eigenstates $\{|\phi_n(Q)\rangle\}$ depend \emph{parametrically} on $Q$ --- $Q$ is treated as a fixed classical parameter, not a quantum degree of freedom --- and form a complete orthonormal basis of $\mathcal{H}_e$ at each $Q$:
\begin{equation}
\sum_n |\phi_n(Q)\rangle\langle\phi_n(Q)| \;=\; \hat{\mathbb{1}}_{e},\qquad \langle\phi_m(Q)|\phi_n(Q)\rangle_e = \delta_{mn}. \tag{4.44}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{The exact Born--Huang expansion}

The \emph{exact} molecular wavefunction $|\Psi\rangle \in L^{2}(\mathcal{C},d\mu_W)$ admits a resolution against the parametric electronic basis. Insert the resolution of identity (4.44) at each $Q$:
\begin{equation}
|\Psi\rangle \;=\; \sum_n |\chi_n\rangle \otimes |\phi_n(Q)\rangle, \qquad \chi_n(\Omega,Q) \;\equiv\; \langle\phi_n(Q)|\Psi\rangle_e, \tag{4.45}
\end{equation}
where $\Omega=(\phi,\theta,\chi)$, the electronic inner product is taken at fixed $Q$, and $\chi_n$ are the \textbf{nuclear coefficient functions}. The expansion (4.45) is \textbf{exact}, not an ansatz: it follows from the completeness (4.44) at each $Q$, with the only subtlety being that the basis $|\phi_n(Q)\rangle$ varies parametrically. There is \emph{no} simple tensor factorization $\mathcal{H}_{\mathrm{molecule}} = \mathcal{H}_N \otimes \mathcal{H}_e$ because of this parametric dependence; the Born--Huang expansion~\cite{bornhuang1954} is the rigorous replacement.

\subsubsection{Coupled-channel equations}

Substitute (4.45) into $\hat H_{\mathrm{int}}|\Psi\rangle = E|\Psi\rangle$, project from the left by $\langle\phi_m(Q)|$, and use (4.43):
\begin{equation}
\bigl[\hat T_N + E_n^{\mathrm{el}}(Q) + V_{NN}(Q) + \hat U(Q)\bigr]\chi_n(\Omega,Q) \;+\; \sum_m \hat\Lambda_{nm}\,\chi_m(\Omega,Q) \;=\; E\,\chi_n(\Omega,Q), \tag{4.46}
\end{equation}
where $\hat T_N$ denotes the nuclear (rotational + vibrational) kinetic operator from the first two lines of (4.41). The \textbf{non-adiabatic coupling operators} $\hat\Lambda_{nm}$ arise from the action of $\hat T_N$ (and the rot--electronic couplings through $\hat{\mathbf{L}}_{\mathrm{elec}}$) on the $Q$-parametric dependence of $|\phi_m(Q)\rangle$:
\begin{equation}
\hat\Lambda_{nm} \;\sim\; -\hbar^{2}\,\langle\phi_n|\nabla_Q\phi_m\rangle_e\!\cdot\!\nabla_Q \;-\; \frac{\hbar^{2}}{2}\,\langle\phi_n|\nabla_Q^{2}\phi_m\rangle_e \;+\;\ldots, \tag{4.47}
\end{equation}
with analogous terms from the rotation--electron Coriolis $\hat J_\alpha\hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\alpha}$ kernel.

\subsubsection{Born--Oppenheimer approximation as truncation}

The \textbf{Born--Oppenheimer (BO) approximation} is the diagonal truncation of (4.46): set $\hat\Lambda_{nm}\to 0$ for $m\ne n$ and (optionally) also $\hat\Lambda_{nn}\to 0$. The resulting effective nuclear problem is
\begin{equation}
\bigl[\hat T_N + E_n^{\mathrm{el}}(Q) + V_{NN}(Q) + \hat U(Q)\bigr]\chi_n^{\mathrm{BO}}(\Omega,Q) \;=\; E^{\mathrm{BO}}\,\chi_n^{\mathrm{BO}}(\Omega,Q), \tag{4.48}
\end{equation}
where the \textbf{Born--Oppenheimer potential energy surface} for electronic state $n$ is
\begin{equation}
V_n^{\mathrm{BO}}(Q) \;\equiv\; E_n^{\mathrm{el}}(Q) + V_{NN}(Q) + \hat U(Q). \tag{4.49}
\end{equation}
The exactness of (4.45) and the well-definedness of (4.46) are mathematical facts. BO is the physical assumption that off-diagonal $\hat\Lambda_{nm}$ are small --- justified when electronic gaps are large compared to nuclear kinetic energies, breaking down at conical intersections and avoided crossings, where the parametric variation of $|\phi_n(Q)\rangle$ becomes singular.

\subsubsection{Summary}

\begin{enumerate}
\item (4.41) is the exact non-relativistic quantum molecular Hamiltonian in BF + Eckart + nuclear-COM coordinates, obtained entirely from coordinate derivatives on a flat internal space: metric (4.15) $\to$ measure (4.21) $\to$ inverse (4.22) $\to$ Laplacian blocks (4.29)--(4.31) $\to$ Podolsky reweighting (4.32$'$) and pseudopotential (4.38).
\item The Born--Huang expansion (4.45) is exact, following from completeness of the parametric electronic basis (4.44).
\item The infinite coupled system (4.46) is equivalent to the Schr\"odinger equation $\hat H_{\mathrm{int}}|\Psi\rangle = E|\Psi\rangle$.
\item The Born--Oppenheimer truncation reduces it to (4.48), giving the familiar single-PES picture (4.49).
\end{enumerate}
