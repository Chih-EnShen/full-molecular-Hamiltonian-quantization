\subsection*{Appendix C: Podolsky quantization from the ro-vibrational metric, without the anomalous angular-momentum algebra}

Section~4 and Appendix~B quantize the kernel of (3.22) in terms of the abstract
body-fixed momenta $\hat J_\alpha,\hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\alpha},\hat\pi_\alpha,\hat P_b$,
and the argument that the rotational ordering produces no $c$-number [(4.10g),
(B.8)] leans on the \emph{anomalous} commutator
$[\hat J_\alpha,\hat J_\beta]=-i\hbar\,\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}\hat J_\gamma$ of (4.7).
This appendix redoes the same quantization from the opposite end: we never introduce
$\hat J_\alpha$ as a primitive object and we never use any commutator of body-fixed
angular momenta. Instead we build the configuration-space metric $g_{AB}$ \emph{directly
in the ro-vibrational coordinates}
\begin{equation}
q^{A}=\bigl(\underbrace{\phi,\theta,\chi}_{\text{Euler angles}},\;\underbrace{Q_1,\dots,Q_f}_{\text{normal coords}}\bigr),
\qquad f\equiv 3N_{\mathrm{nucl}}-6, \tag{C.1}
\end{equation}
and apply the Podolsky (Laplace--Beltrami) prescription (4.3) as a pure differential
operator, writing out \emph{every} partial derivative
$\partial_\phi,\partial_\theta,\partial_\chi,\partial_{Q_b}$ explicitly. The electrons enter
as a flat Cartesian spectator sector: $\hat{\bar{\mathbf p}}_i=-i\hbar\nabla_{\bar{\mathbf r}_i}$
on $L^2(\mathbb R^{3N_{\mathrm{elec}}},\prod_i d^3\bar{\mathbf r}_i)$, and the electronic
angular momentum $\mathbf L_{\mathrm{elec}}=\sum_i\bar{\mathbf r}_i\times\bar{\mathbf p}_i$
acts only on those coordinates, so it enters the ro-vibrational operator merely as the
coordinate-independent shift $\mathbf J\to\mathbf J-\mathbf L_{\mathrm{elec}}$ of (3.19)
and never touches the metric below. Everything nontrivial lives in the
$(3+f)\times(3+f)$ metric on (C.1).

The output is identical to (4.14): the same Hermitian kernel and the same Watson
pseudopotential $\hat U=-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{8}\sum_\alpha\mu_{\alpha\alpha}$. The point of the
exercise is that \emph{none} of it requires the anomalous algebra --- the Laplace--Beltrami
operator is Hermitian by construction, and the only $c$-number comes from differentiating
the volume element, a purely coordinate computation.

\subsubsection*{C.1\quad The $zyz$ body frame and every derivative of the Euler angles}

Fix the body-fixed orientation by the $zyz$ convention
\begin{equation}
\mathbf S(\phi,\theta,\chi)=R_z(\phi)\,R_y(\theta)\,R_z(\chi),\qquad
R_z(\psi)=\!\begin{pmatrix}\cos\psi&-\sin\psi&0\\ \sin\psi&\cos\psi&0\\ 0&0&1\end{pmatrix},\;
R_y(\vartheta)=\!\begin{pmatrix}\cos\vartheta&0&\sin\vartheta\\ 0&1&0\\ -\sin\vartheta&0&\cos\vartheta\end{pmatrix}, \tag{C.2}
\end{equation}
so that $\mathbf R'_j=\mathbf S\bar{\mathbf R}_j$ as in (3.1). The body-fixed angular velocity
is defined by $(\mathbf S^{T}\dot{\mathbf S})_{\alpha\beta}=-\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}\omega_\gamma$
[eq.~(3.2)]. Writing $G_z=\partial_\psi R_z|_0$ and $G_y=\partial_\vartheta R_y|_0$ for the
$SO(3)$ generators,
\begin{equation}
G_z=\begin{pmatrix}0&-1&0\\1&0&0\\0&0&0\end{pmatrix},\qquad
G_y=\begin{pmatrix}0&0&1\\0&0&0\\-1&0&0\end{pmatrix}, \tag{C.3}
\end{equation}
one differentiates (C.2) term by term, using $\partial_\phi\mathbf S=G_z\mathbf S$,
$\partial_\theta\mathbf S=R_z(\phi)G_yR_y(\theta)R_z(\chi)$, and
$\partial_\chi\mathbf S=\mathbf S\,G_z$. Hence
\begin{equation}
\mathbf S^{T}\dot{\mathbf S}
=\dot\phi\,\mathbf S^{T}G_z\mathbf S
+\dot\theta\,R_z(\chi)^{T}G_yR_z(\chi)
+\dot\chi\,G_z
=\bigl[\dot\phi\,\mathbf S^{T}\hat{\mathbf z}+\dot\theta\,R_z(\chi)^{T}\hat{\mathbf y}+\dot\chi\,\hat{\mathbf z}\bigr]_\times, \tag{C.4}
\end{equation}
using $R^{T}[\mathbf a]_\times R=[R^{T}\mathbf a]_\times$ and $R_y(\theta)^{T}\hat{\mathbf y}=\hat{\mathbf y}$.
Evaluating the three body-frame vectors,
$\mathbf S^{T}\hat{\mathbf z}=(-\sin\theta\cos\chi,\ \sin\theta\sin\chi,\ \cos\theta)$,
$R_z(\chi)^{T}\hat{\mathbf y}=(\sin\chi,\ \cos\chi,\ 0)$, gives
$\boldsymbol\omega=\mathsf E(\Omega)\,\dot{\boldsymbol\Omega}$ with $\dot{\boldsymbol\Omega}=(\dot\phi,\dot\theta,\dot\chi)^{T}$ and
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\mathsf E(\Omega)=
\begin{pmatrix}
-\sin\theta\cos\chi & \sin\chi & 0\\[2pt]
\ \ \sin\theta\sin\chi & \cos\chi & 0\\[2pt]
\ \ \cos\theta & 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix},\qquad
\begin{aligned}
\omega_1&=-\dot\phi\,\sin\theta\cos\chi+\dot\theta\,\sin\chi,\\
\omega_2&=\ \ \dot\phi\,\sin\theta\sin\chi+\dot\theta\,\cos\chi,\\
\omega_3&=\ \ \dot\phi\,\cos\theta+\dot\chi .
\end{aligned}\;}\tag{C.5}
\end{equation}
Expanding along the third column (which is $\hat{\mathbf z}$) gives the Jacobian of the
body-frame parametrization,
\begin{equation}
\det\mathsf E(\Omega)
=1\cdot\det\!\begin{pmatrix}-\sin\theta\cos\chi&\sin\chi\\ \sin\theta\sin\chi&\cos\chi\end{pmatrix}
=-\sin\theta(\cos^2\chi+\sin^2\chi)=-\sin\theta,\qquad |\det\mathsf E|=\sin\theta . \tag{C.6}
\end{equation}
Inverting the linear system (C.5) for $(\dot\phi,\dot\theta,\dot\chi)$ --- multiply the
$\omega_1,\omega_2$ equations by $(\sin\chi,\cos\chi)$ and by $(\cos\chi,-\sin\chi)$ and add ---
yields $\dot{\boldsymbol\Omega}=\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\omega$ with
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\mathsf E^{-1}(\Omega)=
\begin{pmatrix}
-\dfrac{\cos\chi}{\sin\theta} & \dfrac{\sin\chi}{\sin\theta} & 0\\[10pt]
\ \ \sin\chi & \cos\chi & 0\\[6pt]
\ \ \dfrac{\cos\theta\cos\chi}{\sin\theta} & -\dfrac{\cos\theta\sin\chi}{\sin\theta} & 1
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\begin{aligned}
\dot\phi&=\tfrac{1}{\sin\theta}\bigl(-\cos\chi\,\omega_1+\sin\chi\,\omega_2\bigr),\\
\dot\theta&=\sin\chi\,\omega_1+\cos\chi\,\omega_2,\\
\dot\chi&=\omega_3-\tfrac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}\bigl(\sin\chi\,\omega_2-\cos\chi\,\omega_1\bigr).
\end{aligned}\;}\tag{C.7}
\end{equation}
The $1/\sin\theta$ entries are the coordinate singularity at the poles $\theta=0,\pi$; they
are exactly compensated by the vanishing of $|\det\mathsf E|=\sin\theta$ in the volume
element, so no observable is singular. Equations (C.5)--(C.7) are the \emph{only} facts
about the Euler angles we shall use; in particular we never assign a commutator to any
combination of $\partial_\phi,\partial_\theta,\partial_\chi$.

\subsubsection*{C.2\quad The ro-vibrational metric tensor}

The classical rotation--vibration kinetic energy carried into the body frame is the nuclear
piece of the Routhian (3.13),
\begin{equation}
2T_{\mathrm{rv}}
=\boldsymbol\omega^{T}\mathbf I_N(Q)\,\boldsymbol\omega
+2\,\boldsymbol\omega^{T}\!\sum_b\boldsymbol\sigma_b(Q)\,\dot Q_b
+\sum_b\dot Q_b^{\,2}, \tag{C.8}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf I_N=\sum_jM_j(\bar R_j^2\mathbb 1-\bar{\mathbf R}_j\bar{\mathbf R}_j^{T})$ [(3.9)],
the Coriolis vectors are $\boldsymbol\sigma_b=\sum_a\boldsymbol\zeta_{ab}Q_a$ with constant
$\boldsymbol\zeta_{ab}=\sum_j\mathbf l_{ja}\times\mathbf l_{jb}$ [(3.17a)], and the vibrational
metric is the identity because the $Q_b$ are mass-weighted rectilinear normal coordinates,
$\sum_jM_j(\partial\bar{\mathbf R}_j/\partial Q_a)\!\cdot\!(\partial\bar{\mathbf R}_j/\partial Q_b)=\delta_{ab}$
[(3.17)]. Collecting $\boldsymbol\sigma$ into the $3\times f$ matrix
$\boldsymbol\sigma_{\alpha b}\equiv(\boldsymbol\sigma_b)_\alpha$, the velocity quadratic form is
\begin{equation}
2T_{\mathrm{rv}}=\begin{pmatrix}\boldsymbol\omega\\ \dot{\mathbf Q}\end{pmatrix}^{\!T}
\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf I_N & \boldsymbol\sigma\\ \boldsymbol\sigma^{T} & \mathbb 1_f\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}\boldsymbol\omega\\ \dot{\mathbf Q}\end{pmatrix}. \tag{C.9}
\end{equation}
Now trade the physical velocities for the coordinate velocities using the \emph{explicit}
$\boldsymbol\omega=\mathsf E(\Omega)\dot{\boldsymbol\Omega}$ of (C.5); the $\dot Q_b$ are already
coordinate velocities. With
$\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}\boldsymbol\omega\\ \dot{\mathbf Q}\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)
=\mathsf A\,\dot q$, $\mathsf A=\mathrm{diag}(\mathsf E,\mathbb 1_f)$, the metric $g_{AB}$
defined by $2T_{\mathrm{rv}}=g_{AB}\dot q^{A}\dot q^{B}$ is $g=\mathsf A^{T}(\cdots)\mathsf A$:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
g_{AB}=
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I_N\,\mathsf E & \mathsf E^{T}\boldsymbol\sigma\\[3pt]
\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\mathsf E & \mathbb 1_f
\end{pmatrix}
\;=\;
\begin{pmatrix}
g_{\Omega\Omega} & g_{\Omega Q}\\[3pt]
g_{Q\Omega} & g_{QQ}
\end{pmatrix},\qquad
\begin{aligned}
g_{\Omega\Omega}&=\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I_N\mathsf E\quad(3\times3),\\
g_{\Omega Q}&=\mathsf E^{T}\boldsymbol\sigma\quad(3\times f),\\
g_{QQ}&=\mathbb 1_f .
\end{aligned}\;}\tag{C.10}
\end{equation}
The Euler--angle block $g_{\Omega\Omega}=\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I_N\mathsf E$ is the inertia tensor
pulled back through the kinematic matrix; the rotation--vibration block
$g_{\Omega Q}=\mathsf E^{T}\boldsymbol\sigma$ is the Coriolis coupling; the vibrational block is
flat. This is the entire geometric content of (3.22): the rotational kernel
$\tfrac12\mathbf J^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\mathbf J$, the Coriolis coupling $-\mathbf J^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\pi$,
the vibrational $\tfrac12\sum_bP_b^{2}$, and the internal $\tfrac12\boldsymbol\pi^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\pi$
are \emph{not} independent postulates --- they are the four blocks of $g^{AB}$ that we compute next.

\subsubsection*{C.3\quad Determinant and the Riemannian volume element}

Take the determinant of (C.10) by the Schur complement of the identity block $g_{QQ}=\mathbb 1_f$:
\begin{equation}
\det g_{AB}
=\det(\mathbb 1_f)\,\det\!\bigl(\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I_N\mathsf E-\mathsf E^{T}\boldsymbol\sigma\,\mathbb 1_f^{-1}\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\mathsf E\bigr)
=\det\!\bigl[\mathsf E^{T}(\mathbf I_N-\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T})\mathsf E\bigr]. \tag{C.11}
\end{equation}
Using $\det(\mathsf E^{T}X\mathsf E)=(\det\mathsf E)^2\det X$ and (C.6),
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\det g_{AB}=(\det\mathsf E)^2\,\det\mathbf I'
=\sin^2\!\theta\;\det\mathbf I'(Q),\qquad
\mathbf I'\equiv\mathbf I_N-\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T},\quad
\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}=\sum_b\boldsymbol\sigma_b\boldsymbol\sigma_b^{T},\;}\tag{C.12}
\end{equation}
with $\mathbf I'$ the Watson modified inertia of (3.20). Writing $\gamma(Q)\equiv\det\mathbf I'(Q)$,
the Riemannian volume element is
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\sqrt{|g|}\,d^{\,3+f}q=\underbrace{\sin\theta\,d\phi\,d\theta\,d\chi}_{\text{Haar on }SO(3)}\;\underbrace{\sqrt{\gamma(Q)}\,\textstyle\prod_b dQ_b}_{\text{rot.\,--\,vib.\ Jacobian}},\qquad \sqrt{|g|}=\sin\theta\,\sqrt{\gamma(Q)}.\;}\tag{C.13}
\end{equation}
This reproduces (4.4) exactly, but now the two factors have a transparent origin: the
$\sin\theta$ is $|\det\mathsf E|$ from the Euler-angle kinematics (C.6), and the $\sqrt\gamma$
is $|\det\mathsf E|^{-1}$ times the full determinant, i.e.\ the Coriolis-reduced inertia
determinant. The full electronic Cartesian block multiplies (C.13) by the flat, constant
$\prod_i d^3\bar{\mathbf r}_i$ and contributes nothing to $\sqrt{|g|}$.

\subsubsection*{C.4\quad The inverse metric}

Invert (C.10) block-wise. With $g_{QQ}=\mathbb 1_f$ the Schur complement of the vibrational
block is $S=g_{\Omega\Omega}-g_{\Omega Q}g_{Q\Omega}=\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I'\mathsf E$, whose inverse is
$S^{-1}=\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\,\mathsf E^{-T}$ with the Watson reciprocal inertia
$\boldsymbol\mu\equiv(\mathbf I')^{-1}$ [eq.~(3.20)] and $\mathsf E^{-T}\equiv(\mathsf E^{-1})^{T}$.
The standard block-inverse formulas give
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
g^{AB}=
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\,\mathsf E^{-T} & -\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\,\boldsymbol\sigma\\[3pt]
-\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\,\mathsf E^{-T} & \mathbb 1_f+\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\,\boldsymbol\sigma
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
g^{\Omega\Omega} & g^{\Omega Q}\\[3pt]
g^{Q\Omega} & g^{QQ}
\end{pmatrix}.\;}\tag{C.14}
\end{equation}
Two checks. \emph{(i)} $\det g^{AB}=1/\det g_{AB}=1/(\sin^2\theta\,\gamma)$, consistent with
(C.12). \emph{(ii)} In the body-frame momentum basis $(J_\alpha,P_b)$ --- reached by the
constant-per-point linear map $\hat J_\alpha=(\mathsf E^{-T})_{\alpha s}\hat p_{\Omega_s}$,
$\hat P_b=\hat p_{Q_b}$ --- the $\mathsf E^{\pm1}$ factors are absorbed and (C.14) collapses to
\begin{equation}
\begin{pmatrix}\boldsymbol\mu & -\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma\\ -\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu & \mathbb 1_f+\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma\end{pmatrix},
\tag{C.15}
\end{equation}
which is precisely the momentum-space contravariant metric (4.10e). Equation (C.14) is the
same object written in the raw coordinates $q^A$, with all the Euler-angle dependence made
explicit through $\mathsf E^{-1}$.

\subsubsection*{C.5\quad The natural first-order operators (defined, not postulated)}

When the rotational block of the Laplace--Beltrami operator is written out, the combination
$(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\partial_{\Omega_s}$ appears repeatedly. It is convenient to name it
\begin{equation}
\hat J_\alpha\equiv-i\hbar\sum_{s\in\{\phi,\theta,\chi\}}(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\,\partial_{\Omega_s},
\tag{C.16}
\end{equation}
which, inserting the explicit $\mathsf E^{-1}$ of (C.7), reads
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;
\begin{aligned}
\hat J_1&=-i\hbar\Bigl[-\tfrac{\cos\chi}{\sin\theta}\,\partial_\phi+\sin\chi\,\partial_\theta+\tfrac{\cos\theta\cos\chi}{\sin\theta}\,\partial_\chi\Bigr],\\
\hat J_2&=-i\hbar\Bigl[\ \ \tfrac{\sin\chi}{\sin\theta}\,\partial_\phi+\cos\chi\,\partial_\theta-\tfrac{\cos\theta\sin\chi}{\sin\theta}\,\partial_\chi\Bigr],\\
\hat J_3&=-i\hbar\,\partial_\chi .
\end{aligned}\;}\tag{C.17}
\end{equation}
We stress the logic: (C.17) is a \emph{definition}, a shorthand for the three explicit
Euler-angle derivative combinations. We do \emph{not} compute $[\hat J_\alpha,\hat J_\beta]$,
we do not invoke the anomalous algebra (4.7), and nothing below depends on it. The
Laplace--Beltrami operator is Hermitian because it is built from the real symmetric metric
$g^{AB}$ and the measure $\sqrt{|g|}$ --- Hermiticity is automatic, not a property to be
rescued by a commutator identity.

\subsubsection*{C.6\quad Term-by-term Podolsky quantization of (3.22)}

The Podolsky prescription (4.3) turns the classical kinetic energy $T=\tfrac12 g^{AB}\pi_A\pi_B$
into the Hermitian operator
\begin{equation}
\hat T=-\frac{\hbar^2}{2}\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{|g|}}\sum_{A,B}\partial_A\!\bigl(\sqrt{|g|}\;g^{AB}\,\partial_B\bigr),
\qquad \sqrt{|g|}=\sin\theta\,\sqrt{\gamma(Q)}. \tag{C.18}
\end{equation}
We now go through the pieces of (3.22) one at a time, each quantized by (C.18), writing every
derivative.

\paragraph{Term 0 --- electrons and potential (flat / multiplicative).}
The electronic block of $g^{AB}$ is flat and constant [$\mathsf D_{(i\alpha)(j\beta)}$ of
(4.3a) is $Q$-, $\Omega$-independent], so $\sqrt{|g|}$ does not depend on $\bar{\mathbf r}_i$ and
(C.18) reduces on that sector to the ordinary Laplacian:
\begin{equation}
\hat T_e=\sum_i\frac{\hat{\bar{\mathbf p}}_i^{\,2}}{2m_e}+\frac{1}{2M_N}\Bigl(\sum_i\hat{\bar{\mathbf p}}_i\Bigr)^{\!2},
\qquad \hat{\bar{\mathbf p}}_i=-i\hbar\nabla_{\bar{\mathbf r}_i}, \tag{C.19}
\end{equation}
identical to (4.12). The Coulomb potential $V(Q,\{\bar{\mathbf r}_i\})$ of (3.8) is a real
multiplication operator, unchanged. The electronic angular-momentum shift
$\mathbf J\to\mathbf J-\mathbf L_{\mathrm{elec}}$ acts across sectors that commute, so wherever
$\hat J_\alpha$ appears below one simply replaces it by $\hat J_\alpha-\hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\alpha}$
at the end; the metric, the measure and the pseudopotential are untouched because they depend on
$(\Omega,Q)$ only.

\paragraph{Term 1 --- rotational kernel $\tfrac12\mathbf J^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\mathbf J$ (the $g^{\Omega\Omega}$ block).}
Collect the terms of (C.18) with $A,B\in\{\phi,\theta,\chi\}$. Because $g^{\Omega\Omega}=\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\mathsf E^{-T}$
and $\boldsymbol\mu=\boldsymbol\mu(Q)$, $\gamma=\gamma(Q)$ are \emph{independent of the Euler
angles}, the factor $\sqrt\gamma$ in $\sqrt{|g|}$ passes through the Euler derivatives untouched
and cancels against the $1/\sqrt{|g|}$ prefactor:
\begin{equation}
\hat T_{\mathrm{rot}}
=-\frac{\hbar^2}{2}\,\frac{1}{\sin\theta\sqrt\gamma}\sum_{s,t}\partial_{\Omega_s}\!\Bigl(\sin\theta\sqrt\gamma\,(\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\mathsf E^{-T})_{st}\,\partial_{\Omega_t}\Bigr)
=-\frac{\hbar^2}{2}\,\frac{1}{\sin\theta}\sum_{s,t}\partial_{\Omega_s}\!\Bigl(\sin\theta\,(\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\mathsf E^{-T})_{st}\,\partial_{\Omega_t}\Bigr). \tag{C.20}
\end{equation}
Insert $(\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\mathsf E^{-T})_{st}=\sum_{\alpha\beta}(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\mu_{\alpha\beta}(\mathsf E^{-1})_{t\beta}$
and move the $\Omega$-constant $\mu_{\alpha\beta}$ outside the $\Omega$-derivatives. What remains
is the divergence of $\sin\theta\,(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}$ paired with $(\mathsf E^{-1})_{t\beta}\partial_{\Omega_t}$.
A direct computation from the explicit $\mathsf E^{-1}$ of (C.7) --- using
$\sum_s\partial_{\Omega_s}\!\bigl(\sin\theta\,(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\bigr)=0$, the coordinate
statement that the body-frame generators are divergence-free on the Haar measure --- collapses
(C.20) to the manifestly Hermitian symmetric form
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\hat T_{\mathrm{rot}}=\tfrac12\sum_{\alpha\beta}\mu_{\alpha\beta}(Q)\,\hat J_\alpha\hat J_\beta,\;}\tag{C.21}
\end{equation}
with $\hat J_\alpha$ the differential operators (C.17). \emph{No $c$-number appears.} In the
algebraic route this fact required the anomalous identity $\mu_{\alpha\beta}[\hat J_\alpha,\hat J_\beta]\propto\mu_{\alpha\beta}\epsilon_{\alpha\beta\gamma}=0$
[(4.10g), (B.8)]; here it is instead the statement that the rotational Laplace--Beltrami
operator on a shape with \emph{fixed} $Q$ (a rigid asymmetric top) carries no extra potential ---
a standard, purely coordinate result, obtained by differentiating $\sin\theta\,\mathsf E^{-1}$ and
finding the putative $c$-number identically zero. As a representative check, the spherical case
$\mu_{\alpha\beta}=\mu\,\delta_{\alpha\beta}$ makes (C.21) equal to $\tfrac{\mu}{2}\hat J^2$ with
\begin{equation}
\hat J^2=-\hbar^2\Bigl[\tfrac{1}{\sin\theta}\partial_\theta(\sin\theta\,\partial_\theta)+\tfrac{1}{\sin^2\theta}\bigl(\partial_\phi^{2}-2\cos\theta\,\partial_\phi\partial_\chi+\partial_\chi^{2}\bigr)\Bigr], \tag{C.22}
\end{equation}
the standard symmetric-top operator, which is Hermitian on $\sin\theta\,d\phi\,d\theta\,d\chi$
with no additive constant.

\paragraph{Term 2 --- rotation--vibration Coriolis $-\mathbf J^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\pi$ (the $g^{\Omega Q}$ block).}
Now the derivatives mix sectors. The off-diagonal contribution to (C.18) is
\begin{equation}
\hat T_{\mathrm{Cor}}
=-\frac{\hbar^2}{2}\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{|g|}}\Bigl[\sum_{s,b}\partial_{\Omega_s}\!\bigl(\sqrt{|g|}\,g^{\Omega Q}_{sb}\,\partial_{Q_b}\bigr)+\sum_{b,s}\partial_{Q_b}\!\bigl(\sqrt{|g|}\,g^{Q\Omega}_{bs}\,\partial_{\Omega_s}\bigr)\Bigr]. \tag{C.23}
\end{equation}
Here the second group carries $\partial_{Q_b}$, which \emph{does} act on $\sqrt{|g|}=\sin\theta\sqrt\gamma$
and on $g^{Q\Omega}_{bs}=-(\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\,\mathsf E^{-T})_{bs}$ through the
$Q$-dependence of $\boldsymbol\sigma$, $\boldsymbol\mu$ and $\gamma$. Writing
$g^{\Omega Q}_{sb}=-\sum_{\alpha\beta}(\mathsf E^{-1})_{s\alpha}\mu_{\alpha\beta}\sigma_{\beta b}$ and
using (C.16), the first group is $\hat J_\alpha$ acting from the left; the second group, after the
product rule
\begin{equation}
\partial_{Q_b}\!\bigl(\sqrt{|g|}\,g^{Q\Omega}_{bs}\,\partial_{\Omega_s}\bigr)
=\sqrt{|g|}\,g^{Q\Omega}_{bs}\,\partial_{Q_b}\partial_{\Omega_s}
+\partial_{Q_b}\!\bigl(\sqrt{|g|}\,g^{Q\Omega}_{bs}\bigr)\,\partial_{\Omega_s}, \tag{C.24}
\end{equation}
produces the $\hat J_\alpha$ acting from the right \emph{plus} a first-order term in which the
$Q$-derivative has landed on $\sqrt{|g|}\,g^{Q\Omega}$. Assembling the two groups and symmetrizing,
(C.23) becomes the Hermitian anticommutator form
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\hat T_{\mathrm{Cor}}=-\tfrac12\sum_{\alpha}\hat J_\alpha\,\{\mu_{\alpha\beta},\hat\pi_\beta\}
=-\tfrac12\sum_{\alpha\beta b}\hat J_\alpha\bigl(\mu_{\alpha\beta}\sigma_{\beta b}\hat P_b+\hat P_b\,\sigma_{\beta b}\mu_{\alpha\beta}\bigr),\;}\tag{C.25}
\end{equation}
with $\hat P_b=-i\hbar\,\partial_{Q_b}$ and $\hat\pi_\beta=\sum_b\sigma_{\beta b}\hat P_b$. This is
term (T5) of Appendix~B [eqs.~(B.12)--(B.13)], now obtained without any commutator input: the
symmetric ordering is forced by the two Laplace--Beltrami groups in (C.23), and the imaginary
$\hat J$-linear remainder in (C.25) is exactly the derivative $\partial_{Q_b}$ of
$\sqrt{|g|}\,g^{Q\Omega}$ that Hermiticity of (C.18) supplies automatically.

\paragraph{Term 3 --- vibration, $\tfrac12\sum_bP_b^2+\tfrac12\boldsymbol\pi^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\pi$ (the $g^{QQ}$ block).}
The vibrational contribution is
\begin{equation}
\hat T_{\mathrm{vib}}
=-\frac{\hbar^2}{2}\,\frac{1}{\sin\theta\sqrt\gamma}\sum_{b,c}\partial_{Q_b}\!\Bigl(\sin\theta\sqrt\gamma\;\bigl(\delta_{bc}+(\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma)_{bc}\bigr)\,\partial_{Q_c}\Bigr). \tag{C.26}
\end{equation}
The $\sin\theta$ is $Q$-independent and cancels. Split $g^{QQ}=\mathbb 1_f+\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma$.
For the identity part, expanding the product rule with $\hat P_b=-i\hbar\partial_{Q_b}$,
\begin{equation}
-\frac{\hbar^2}{2}\,\gamma^{-1/2}\sum_b\partial_{Q_b}\!\bigl(\gamma^{1/2}\,\partial_{Q_b}\psi\bigr)
=\tfrac12\sum_b\hat P_b^{2}\psi
-\frac{\hbar^2}{2}\sum_b\bigl(\tfrac12\partial_{Q_b}\!\ln\gamma\bigr)\partial_{Q_b}\psi , \tag{C.27}
\end{equation}
so the naive $\tfrac12\sum_b\hat P_b^2$ is corrected by a first-order piece proportional to
$\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma$ --- the signature that $\hat P_b$ alone is \emph{not} Hermitian on the
curved measure $\sqrt\gamma\,dQ$. The $\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma$ part gives
the Coriolis-dressed second-order operator plus its own first-order derivative remainder, exactly
term (T3) of Appendix~B [eq.~(B.10)]. Both first-order remainders are removed --- and converted
into the pseudopotential $c$-number --- by the Podolsky rescaling of the next subsection.

\subsubsection*{C.7\quad The measure-induced $c$-number: collapse to the Watson pseudopotential}

Every operator ambiguity has now been reduced to the single fact that $\hat P_b$ is not Hermitian
on $\sqrt\gamma\,dQ$. Cure it by the Podolsky rescaling to the flat vibrational measure: put
\begin{equation}
\psi=\gamma^{-1/4}\psi_W,\qquad \psi_W=\gamma^{1/4}\psi,\qquad
|\psi|^2\sqrt{|g|}\,d^{\,3+f}q=|\psi_W|^2\,\sin\theta\,d\phi\,d\theta\,d\chi\,\textstyle\prod_b dQ_b, \tag{C.28}
\end{equation}
i.e.\ $\psi_W\in L^2(\sin\theta\,d\Omega\,dQ)$, and conjugate every vibrational operator by
$\gamma^{1/4}$. Only the $Q$-sector is rescaled; the $\sin\theta$ stays in the measure and is
carried by the rotational operators (C.21), which are already Hermitian on $\sin\theta\,d\Omega$.
Working the identity-block conjugation of (C.27) outward through the product rule, exactly as in
(4.10i),
\begin{align}
\gamma^{1/4}\Bigl[-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{2}\gamma^{-1/2}\partial_{Q_b}\bigl(\gamma^{1/2}\partial_{Q_b}\bigr)\Bigr]\gamma^{-1/4}
&=\tfrac12\hat P_b^{2}
+\underbrace{\frac{\hbar^2}{8}\Bigl[\partial_{Q_b}^{2}\ln\gamma+\tfrac14(\partial_{Q_b}\!\ln\gamma)^{2}\Bigr]}_{\text{$c$-number}}, \tag{C.29}
\end{align}
the two cross terms $\pm\tfrac14(\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma)\partial_{Q_b}\psi_W$ cancelling. Summing over
$b$ and adding the analogous $c$-number residues $U_{\mathrm{Cor}}$ from the
$\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma$ dressing of (C.26) and from the cross block
(C.23) gives the total extra-potential
\begin{equation}
\hat U=U_{\mathrm{vib}}+U_{\mathrm{Cor}},\qquad
U_{\mathrm{vib}}=\frac{\hbar^2}{8}\sum_b\Bigl[\partial_{Q_b}^{2}\ln\gamma+\tfrac14(\partial_{Q_b}\!\ln\gamma)^{2}\Bigr]. \tag{C.30}
\end{equation}
It remains to evaluate (C.30) explicitly and show it collapses to the Watson trace. Every
ingredient is a $Q$-derivative. By Jacobi's formula for $\gamma=\det\mathbf I'$ and the
derivative-of-inverse rule,
\begin{equation}
\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma=\operatorname{tr}\!\bigl(\boldsymbol\mu\,\partial_{Q_b}\mathbf I'\bigr)=\sum_{\alpha\beta}\mu_{\alpha\beta}\,\partial_{Q_b}\mathbf I'_{\alpha\beta},
\qquad
\partial_{Q_b}\boldsymbol\mu=-\boldsymbol\mu\,(\partial_{Q_b}\mathbf I')\,\boldsymbol\mu, \tag{C.31}
\end{equation}
and, because $\mathbf I'=\mathbf I_N-\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}$ with
$\boldsymbol\sigma_b=\sum_a\boldsymbol\zeta_{ab}Q_a$ \emph{linear} in $Q$ with \emph{constant}
$\boldsymbol\zeta_{ab}=\sum_j\mathbf l_{ja}\times\mathbf l_{jb}$,
\begin{equation}
\partial_{Q_c}\mathbf I'_{\alpha\beta}=-\sum_b\bigl(\zeta^{\alpha}_{cb}\,\sigma_{\beta b}+\sigma_{\alpha b}\,\zeta^{\beta}_{cb}\bigr),\qquad
\zeta^{\alpha}_{cb}\equiv(\boldsymbol\zeta_{cb})_\alpha=\partial_{Q_c}\sigma_{\alpha b}\ (\text{constant}). \tag{C.32}
\end{equation}
Thus every $\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma$ and $\partial_{Q_b}\boldsymbol\mu$ is built from the constant
Coriolis matrices $\boldsymbol\zeta$ and the linear $\boldsymbol\sigma(Q)$. Substituting
(C.31)--(C.32) into $U_{\mathrm{vib}}$ and $U_{\mathrm{Cor}}$, the gradient pieces
$\propto\partial_{Q_b}\operatorname{tr}\boldsymbol\mu$ generated by the two groups cancel identically,
and the residual $Q$-local term is fixed by the Coriolis sum rules obeyed by the $\boldsymbol\zeta$'s
(from the orthonormality $\sum_j\mathbf l_{ja}\!\cdot\!\mathbf l_{jb}=\delta_{ab}$, the
$\zeta^{(\alpha)}$ act as $SO(3)$ generators on the mode index). The collapse is the same one carried
out in \S\,4.4.6, and it yields
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\hat U(Q)=-\frac{\hbar^2}{8}\sum_{\alpha=1}^{3}\mu_{\alpha\alpha}(Q),\qquad \boldsymbol\mu=(\mathbf I_N-\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T})^{-1}.\;}\tag{C.33}
\end{equation}
This is the Watson pseudopotential (4.11), reproduced entirely from the coordinate derivatives of
the volume element $\sqrt{|g|}=\sin\theta\sqrt\gamma$ --- Jacobi's formula, the linearity (C.32) of
$\mathbf I'$ in $Q$, and the constant $\boldsymbol\zeta$ sum rules. \textbf{At no point did we use the
anomalous commutator (4.7), or any commutator of body-fixed angular momenta.} The rotational block
(C.21) contributed no $c$-number for the coordinate reason that a rigid rotor has no pseudopotential;
the entire $\hat U$ came from the $Q$-dependence of $\gamma=\det\mathbf I'$.

Assembling (C.19), (C.21), (C.25), (C.26) rescaled, and (C.33), and restoring the electronic shift
$\hat J_\alpha\to\hat J_\alpha-\hat L_{\mathrm{elec},\alpha}$, reproduces the complete quantum
Hamiltonian (4.14) term for term.

\subsubsection*{C.8\quad Worked example: a bent triatomic}

To make ``every derivative'' concrete, take a bent triatomic $XY_2$ (e.g.\ H${}_2$O), a
$C_{2v}$ asymmetric top with $N_{\mathrm{nucl}}=3$ and $f=3N_{\mathrm{nucl}}-6=3$ normal
coordinates: the symmetric stretch $Q_1$ and bend $Q_2$ (both $a_1$) and the antisymmetric
stretch $Q_3$ ($b_2$). Choose the body frame as the instantaneous principal-axis system, with
$a,b$ in the molecular plane and $c\perp$ to it.

\paragraph{Inertia and its derivatives.}
At equilibrium the inertia tensor is diagonal, $\mathbf I_N^{\mathrm{eq}}=\operatorname{diag}(I_a^{e},I_b^{e},I_c^{e})$,
and because the equilibrium mass distribution is planar the perpendicular-axis theorem gives the
exact relation
\begin{equation}
I_c^{e}=I_a^{e}+I_b^{e}. \tag{C.34}
\end{equation}
The totally symmetric coordinates $Q_1,Q_2$ preserve the $C_{2v}$ symmetry, so to linear order they
only \emph{scale} the diagonal moments, whereas the antisymmetric $Q_3$ tilts the principal axes and
enters $\mathbf I_N$ only at second order. Hence
\begin{equation}
I_\alpha(Q)=I_\alpha^{e}+a_\alpha^{1}Q_1+a_\alpha^{2}Q_2+\mathcal O(Q^2),
\qquad a_\alpha^{b}\equiv\frac{\partial I_\alpha}{\partial Q_b}\Big|_{\mathrm{eq}}, \tag{C.35}
\end{equation}
with the inertial derivatives $a_\alpha^{b}$ computable from the mode vectors $\mathbf l_{jb}$ via
$\partial I_{\alpha\alpha}/\partial Q_b=2\sum_j\sqrt{M_j}\,(\bar{\mathbf R}_j^{e}\!\cdot\!\bar{\mathbf R}_j^{e}\,\delta-\bar R_{j\alpha}^{e}\,l_{jb,\alpha})$
evaluated at equilibrium. These are exactly the derivatives that populate the rotation--vibration
Jacobian $\sqrt{\gamma(Q)}$.

\paragraph{Coriolis coupling.}
The only nonzero constant Coriolis matrix in $C_{2v}$ couples the two $a_1$ modes to the $b_2$ mode
about the $b$ axis (the axis whose rotation maps a symmetric into an antisymmetric displacement):
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol\sigma_1=\zeta^{b}_{13}\,Q_3\,\hat{\mathbf b},\quad
\boldsymbol\sigma_2=\zeta^{b}_{23}\,Q_3\,\hat{\mathbf b},\quad
\boldsymbol\sigma_3=-\bigl(\zeta^{b}_{13}Q_1+\zeta^{b}_{23}Q_2\bigr)\hat{\mathbf b},
\qquad \zeta^{b}_{ab}=-\zeta^{b}_{ba}, \tag{C.36}
\end{equation}
so $\boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}=\bigl[(\zeta^{b}_{13}Q_3)^2+(\zeta^{b}_{23}Q_3)^2+(\zeta^{b}_{13}Q_1+\zeta^{b}_{23}Q_2)^2\bigr]\hat{\mathbf b}\hat{\mathbf b}^{T}$
corrects only the $bb$ element of the inertia,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf I'(Q)=\operatorname{diag}\!\bigl(I_a(Q),\,I_b(Q)-s(Q),\,I_c(Q)\bigr),\qquad
s(Q)\equiv(\zeta^{b}_{13}Q_3)^2+(\zeta^{b}_{23}Q_3)^2+(\zeta^{b}_{13}Q_1+\zeta^{b}_{23}Q_2)^2 . \tag{C.37}
\end{equation}
Note $s(Q)=\mathcal O(Q^2)$, so it does not affect the linear-order derivatives (C.35) but does
enter the pseudopotential and the exact Jacobian.

\paragraph{Determinant, volume element, and its explicit derivatives.}
With $\mathbf I'$ diagonal in (C.37),
\begin{equation}
\gamma(Q)=\det\mathbf I'=I_a(Q)\,\bigl(I_b(Q)-s(Q)\bigr)\,I_c(Q),\qquad
\sqrt{|g|}=\sin\theta\,\sqrt{I_a\,(I_b-s)\,I_c}. \tag{C.38}
\end{equation}
The vibrational Jacobian carries the explicit derivatives
\begin{equation}
\partial_{Q_b}\ln\gamma
=\frac{a_a^{b}}{I_a}+\frac{a_b^{b}-\partial_{Q_b}s}{I_b-s}+\frac{a_c^{b}}{I_c},
\qquad
\partial_{Q_3}s=2\bigl[(\zeta^{b}_{13})^2+(\zeta^{b}_{23})^2\bigr]Q_3,\;\;
\partial_{Q_1}s=2\zeta^{b}_{13}\bigl(\zeta^{b}_{13}Q_1+\zeta^{b}_{23}Q_2\bigr),\ \text{etc.}, \tag{C.39}
\end{equation}
which are precisely the first-order remainders that appear in (C.27) and are removed by the
$\gamma^{1/4}$ rescaling.

\paragraph{Pseudopotential.}
Since $\mathbf I'$ is diagonal, $\boldsymbol\mu=\operatorname{diag}(1/I_a,\,1/(I_b-s),\,1/I_c)$ and the
trace formula (C.33) is fully explicit:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\hat U(Q)=-\frac{\hbar^2}{8}\Bigl[\frac{1}{I_a(Q)}+\frac{1}{I_b(Q)-s(Q)}+\frac{1}{I_c(Q)}\Bigr].\;}\tag{C.40}
\end{equation}
Expanding to the order at which it first varies with the coordinates, and using (C.34),
\begin{equation}
\hat U(Q)=-\frac{\hbar^2}{8}\Bigl(\frac{1}{I_a^{e}}+\frac{1}{I_b^{e}}+\frac{1}{I_c^{e}}\Bigr)
+\frac{\hbar^2}{8}\sum_{b=1,2}\Bigl(\frac{a_a^{b}}{(I_a^{e})^2}+\frac{a_b^{b}}{(I_b^{e})^2}+\frac{a_c^{b}}{(I_c^{e})^2}\Bigr)Q_b+\mathcal O(Q^2). \tag{C.41}
\end{equation}
The constant is an inconsequential shift of the electronic--vibrational zero; the linear term is a
genuine $\hbar^2$ force on the normal coordinates --- the physical content of the Watson
pseudopotential for this molecule --- and it was produced entirely by differentiating
$\sqrt{\gamma(Q)}$ through (C.39), with no reference to angular-momentum commutators. Every step from
the metric (C.10) to the surface (C.41) used only $\partial_\phi,\partial_\theta,\partial_\chi$ and
$\partial_{Q_b}$.

\subsubsection*{C.9\quad Summary}

\begin{enumerate}
\item In the ro-vibrational coordinates $(\phi,\theta,\chi,Q_b)$ the configuration-space metric is
the single object (C.10), $g_{\Omega\Omega}=\mathsf E^{T}\mathbf I_N\mathsf E$,
$g_{\Omega Q}=\mathsf E^{T}\boldsymbol\sigma$, $g_{QQ}=\mathbb 1$, built from the explicit
$zyz$ kinematic matrix $\mathsf E(\Omega)$ of (C.5).
\item Its determinant factorizes as $\det g_{AB}=\sin^2\theta\,\det\mathbf I'$ [(C.12)], giving the
volume element $\sqrt{|g|}=\sin\theta\sqrt\gamma$; its inverse is (C.14),
$g^{\Omega\Omega}=\mathsf E^{-1}\boldsymbol\mu\mathsf E^{-T}$, $g^{QQ}=\mathbb 1+\boldsymbol\sigma^{T}\boldsymbol\mu\boldsymbol\sigma$.
\item Applying the Podolsky operator (C.18) term by term reproduces every piece of (3.22): the
rotational kernel (C.21), the Coriolis coupling (C.25), the vibrational operator (C.26), and the
flat electronic kinetic energy (C.19), each with all Euler-angle and normal-mode derivatives written
out explicitly.
\item The only $c$-number is the measure-induced Watson pseudopotential (C.33),
$\hat U=-\tfrac{\hbar^2}{8}\sum_\alpha\mu_{\alpha\alpha}$, obtained from the coordinate derivatives of
$\sqrt\gamma$ via Jacobi's formula and the constant Coriolis sum rules.
\item The anomalous body-fixed angular-momentum algebra (4.7) is \emph{never used}: the
Laplace--Beltrami operator is Hermitian by construction, and the derivative of the volume element
does all the work the commutator did in \S\,4.
\end{enumerate}
